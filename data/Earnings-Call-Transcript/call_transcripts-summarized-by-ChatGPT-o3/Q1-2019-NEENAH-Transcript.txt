# Neenah, Inc. – Q1 2019 Earnings Call Summary  

Neenah’s first quarter of 2019 unfolded much as management had cautioned in February: it was the most difficult quarter of the year, pressured by still-elevated input costs, a weaker euro and soft demand in several overseas markets.  Even so, the company confirmed that price actions, cost controls and a visible pipeline of higher-margin growth projects—led by transportation filtration, digital transfer papers and premium packaging—should lift revenue, margins, and cash generation as 2019 progresses.

Revenue, Earnings and Major Drivers  
• Consolidated net sales were $240 million.  After stripping out the late-2018 divestiture of the Brattleboro, Vermont mill and currency translation, sales declined 5 percent year-on-year.  
• Diluted EPS were $0.69 versus $0.95 a year earlier.  The current quarter contained no non-recurring charges, whereas Q1 2018 included $0.09 per share of tax-law-related and pension-settlement adjustments.  
• Input costs remained high: they were almost $8 million above prior-year levels, but $6 million of this was offset by price increases.  
• Adverse currency movements trimmed about $6 million from revenue and $1 million from operating income.  
• Management has already implemented price increases that are projected to add roughly $25 million in 2019—sufficient to recoup the $10 million of cost inflation left uncovered in 2018 plus an estimated $15 million of additional inflation this year.  Roughly one-quarter of that benefit ($6 million) was realized in Q1; the incremental benefit is expected to flow evenly over the remaining quarters.  

Segment Performance  
Technical Products  
• Sales fell to $140 million from $154 million.  Roughly half of the decline was currency; constant-currency sales were down 5 percent amid weaker demand in Europe and Asia.  
• Backings for industrial tapes and abrasives—Neenah’s most cyclical product line—accounted for most of the shortfall.  A strong U.S. dollar blunted export competitiveness, and some customers utilized their own idle capacity.  
• Transportation filtration sales were flat in constant currency; 70 percent growth in U.S. volumes offset softness in Asia and Europe.  Qualification of new grades on the new U.S. asset is on track and should contribute about $15 million of incremental filtration revenue this year, supporting the business’s long-term 7-8 percent compound growth target.  
• Operating income dropped $6 million year-on-year to $11.3 million, hurt by lower volume, under-absorbed fixed costs, $4 million of higher inputs and $1 million of currency headwind.  Higher selling prices, a richer mix and lower SG&A and distribution expense partially offset the pressure.  

Fine Paper & Packaging  
• Revenue declined $12 million to $100 million.  Half of the decrease stemmed from the Brattleboro divestiture; the balance reflected the continuing contraction of commercial printing grades, partly offset by higher pricing.  
• Operating income of $12 million was $1 million below the prior year.  Approximately $4 million of higher pulp costs were not yet fully recovered, although management reiterated that the announced price increases will more than offset pulp inflation for the full year.  Lower segment SG&A and the absence of low-margin Brattleboro sales provided some relief.  

Corporate Items, Cash Flow and Balance Sheet  
• Consolidated SG&A was trimmed to $25 million from $27 million; unallocated corporate expense fell to $5.8 million.  The full-year SG&A outlook has been reduced by $4 million to a run-rate of about $25 million per quarter.  
• Interest expense was $3.2 million, marginally below last year’s $3.3 million thanks to lower average debt.  
• The effective tax rate was 17 percent (vs. 22 percent); management continues to guide to a full-year book rate of no more than 22 percent and a mid-teens cash tax rate, aided by R&D credits.  
• Operating cash flow was $3 million, down $5 million year-over-year, reflecting lower earnings and seasonal working-capital needs.  Capital spending was prudently reduced to $4 million—50 percent below Q1 2018—and is expected to finish the year at roughly 3 percent of sales, the low end of Neenah’s targeted range.  
• Net debt ended the quarter at $246 million, down from $272 million a year ago.  The capital structure is dominated by $175 million of unsecured U.S. notes maturing in 2021; all debt is now pre-payable without penalty, giving the company flexibility to refinance or retire the notes ahead of maturity.  

Strategic and Operating Outlook  
Management reiterated that Q1 should mark the trough.  Price increases are now fully in market, raw-material indices—pulp in particular—are retreating, and disciplined SG&A and CapEx controls are in place.  Filtration, digital transfer papers (bolstered by the Coldenhove acquisition) and premium packaging remain the principal growth engines.  Digital transfer continues to grow in the mid- to high-single-digit range, is running ahead of the original acquisition model, and requires minimal incremental capital because the Dutch mill still has latent capacity.  

On a full-year basis Neenah expects:  
• Top-line improvement as filtration volumes ramp and pricing actions annualize;  
• Margin expansion as cost inflation moderates and pulp prices ease;  
• Capital spending limited to high-return cost-reduction or capacity projects (≈3 percent of sales); and  
• Free cash flow prioritized first to organic investment, then dividends, debt reduction and opportunistic share repurchases.  

Management stressed that its long-term value-creation framework—capital-efficient growth, disciplined asset management, and a balanced capital-allocation strategy—remains intact.  Although macro visibility is still cloudy, especially in Europe and Asia, the company believes the actions under its control will allow it to exit 2019 with healthier margins, stronger cash generation and a solid platform for renewed earnings growth in 2020.
