# 3M Company – Q1 2020 Earnings Call Summary  

3M opened 2020 with a solid first-quarter financial performance and detailed its multi-faceted response to COVID-19, while also explaining why it is withdrawing full-year guidance and focusing on liquidity preservation and cost control.

First-quarter GAAP revenue reached $8.08 billion, up 2.7 percent year on year and $219 million above consensus; organic local-currency sales grew 0.3 percent. Adjusted EPS of $2.16 exceeded consensus by $0.15 but was 3 percent below the prior-year period. The company generated adjusted operating income of $1.69 billion, producing an adjusted operating margin of 20.8 percent (about 21 percent). Margin movements were shaped by 40 bps of underlying productivity and 40 bps of price/raw-material benefit, offset by a 40-bp COVID-related asset write-down and a 90-bp drag from acquisitions/divestitures (principally Acelity).

Cash remains a point of strength: adjusted free cash flow was roughly $0.9 billion, with 74 percent conversion of net income. 3M ended March with $4.5 billion of cash and marketable securities (bolstered by a $1.75 billion debt issue in March) and faces two 2020 maturities that total $1.2 billion. A sale of the drug-delivery business is expected to close in Q2 and provide another ~$400 million after tax. Capex guidance has been cut from $1.6–$1.8 billion to about $1.3 billion, which still includes an incremental $100 million devoted to respirator capacity expansion. Share repurchases were suspended on 20 March, but the dividend remains a top capital-allocation priority.

Segment and end-market trends were decidedly mixed. Strength appeared in personal safety (+14 percent organically, helped by N95 respirators), home improvement, retail cleaning, food safety and biopharma filtration. Weakness intensified in oral care, automotive, aerospace and general industrial, especially after mid-March shutdowns. Geographically, Asia-Pacific—China in particular—was hit first but showed sequential improvement through March; the Americas (the U.S. still grew 4 percent in Q1) and EMEA decelerated sharply late in the quarter.

Management highlighted several headline items:

• N95 respirators had represented about 2 percent of total company sales (~$600 million annually pre-crisis). By January, 3M had already doubled annual respirator output to 1.1 billion units (100 million per month globally, 35 million in the U.S.). Working with the U.S. Department of Defense, the company plans to double again to roughly 2 billion units annually by year-end; U.S. production will climb to ~50 million per month in June (a 40 percent sequential increase). 3M estimates that respirators alone should add about 150 basis points to total-company organic growth in Q2.  
• COVID-19 asset write-downs, stemming mainly from the new-ventures portfolio, cost 40 bps of Q1 margin and $0.04 of EPS.  
• 3M has imposed a global hiring freeze, curtailed temporary-worker usage, sharply cut indirect spending and implemented furloughs where demand or government orders have forced plant suspensions. These actions, along with 2019 restructuring carry-over, are expected to yield $350–$400 million of cost savings in Q2; management projects Q2 decremental margins of 30–40 percent despite those savings.  
• Roughly 75 percent of plants and distribution centers were fully or partially operational as of late April. Enterprise Operations—3M’s new integrated manufacturing, supply-chain and customer-service model—has allowed production-planning cycle times to be reduced by 70 percent, helping inventories continue to move lower even amid volatility.  
• Liquidity is further supported by a robust global credit line ($4.25 billion revolving facility maturing November 2023) that remains undrawn. Pension plans, which were well funded at year-end 2019, saw only a minor hit in Q1; 3M still expects to contribute about $200 million to defined-benefit plans in 2020.  
• The company withdrew its full-year 2020 outlook, citing limited visibility on COVID-19’s duration and economic impact. April-to-date organic sales are running down in the mid-teens percent year on year—strong respirator demand notwithstanding—so management expects both revenue and EPS to decline in Q2. 3M will publish monthly revenue updates (by business and geography) until conditions normalize sufficiently to reinstate formal guidance.

Divisional highlights showed how uneven the quarter was:

– Safety & Industrial grew, with personal-safety equipment offsetting weakness in electrical markets, abrasives and general industrial. Operating margins improved meaningfully thanks to spending discipline and last year’s restructuring actions.  
– Health Care delivered slightly positive organic growth (~+1 percent), led by medical solutions and food safety. Elective-procedure deferrals and a steep fall in oral-care demand hurt late in the quarter; the Acelity acquisition reduced divisional operating margin but EBITDA disclosure now provided by management should help investors track recovery.  
– Transportation & Electronics faced declines in automotive OEM and consumer electronics but benefited from resilient semiconductor manufacturing; the segment declined modestly overall.  
– Consumer saw solid demand for home improvement and cleaning products, but stationery/office weakened markedly as schools and offices shut.

Management reiterated its three near-term priorities: safeguard employees, fight the pandemic “from all angles,” and maintain business continuity so the firm can lead out of the slowdown. 3M has pledged $20 million in cash to support health-care workers and vulnerable populations, has joined with external partners (e.g., Ford, Cummins) to scale powered air-purifying respirators, and is aggressively combating price-gouging and counterfeit products through litigation, distributor terminations and cooperation with law-enforcement agencies.

In sum, 3M entered the crisis with a strong balance sheet, diverse portfolio and ample liquidity. Q1 results demonstrated operational resilience, but the company is bracing for a difficult second quarter marked by mid-teens organic sales declines, 30–40 percent decremental margins and elevated uncertainty. Expanded N95 capacity, aggressive cost controls, curtailed capital spending and a suspended buyback position the firm to protect cash, preserve its dividend and emerge in a stronger competitive posture once end-markets recover.
