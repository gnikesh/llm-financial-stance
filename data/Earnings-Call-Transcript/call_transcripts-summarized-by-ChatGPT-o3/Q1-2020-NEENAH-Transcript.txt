# Neenah, Inc. – Q1 2020 Earnings Call Summary  

Neenah delivered a solid first quarter, with profitability and cash generation improving even as the coronavirus began to weigh on demand.  Consolidated net sales declined 3 percent year on year to $234 million, but adjusted earnings per share surged 62 percent to $1.12 (GAAP EPS $0.97, up 40 percent).  Adjusted operating income rose to $27 million from $17 million, lifting the consolidated operating margin, while return on invested capital moved back into double-digit territory.  The quarter included $3.5 million of unusual costs: a $1 million one-time COVID-19 payment to hourly employees, roughly $1 million of costs tied to the terminated Vectorply acquisition, and $1.4 million of restructuring charges linked to the new functional organization.

Segment performance diverged.  Technical Products posted sales of $142 million, up 2 percent (up 3 percent in constant currency) on 7 percent volume growth.  Transportation and other filtration media grew in the low- to mid-single digits and performance materials recorded double-digit gains in backings, dye-sublimation and labels.  Higher volume, better fixed-cost absorption and lower input costs expanded the segment’s adjusted operating margin to 12 percent versus 8 percent a year ago, lifting adjusted operating income by almost $6 million to $17 million.  Fine Paper & Packaging sales fell 8 percent to $91 million as commercial print volume dropped more than 10 percent and a distributor transition continued; nonetheless, premium packaging labels and ASTROBRIGHTS® consumer papers both grew at a double-digit rate.  Cost savings and lower pulp and other inputs allowed the segment to sustain an operating margin in the upper-teens and lift adjusted operating income nearly $4 million to about $16 million.

Cash flow and liquidity were strong.  Operating cash flow was $14 million versus $3 million in 2019 despite a $14 million working-capital build, and capital spending held at $5 million.  In late March the company drew $65 million on its asset-based revolver, finishing the quarter with $78 million of cash, $272 million of total debt (net debt just under $200 million) and a net-debt-to-EBITDA ratio of 1.5×.  Including $117 million of remaining revolver availability, liquidity stood just shy of $200 million.  The balance-sheet remains anchored by a $175 million senior unsecured note maturing in May 2021; refinancing plans have been postponed until market terms improve, but management is comfortable with its ability to renew the debt later this year.

Management moved quickly to preserve cash in light of COVID-19.  Planned cost-saving and cash-generation measures total more than $50 million: SG&A has been pared to roughly $23 million per quarter; capital spending for 2020 has been halved to about $15 million; pension contributions will drop $6 million to roughly $7 million; share repurchases are suspended; payroll and other tax payments are being deferred under the CARES Act; and working-capital efficiencies are being pursued aggressively.  A lower-capability fine-paper machine in Wisconsin was idled in early May, a move expected to save over $1 million annually from the second half, although a largely non-cash charge of about $2 million will be taken in Q2.

Even amid the downturn, Neenah continues to invest in R&D and new product platforms.  The company has already secured orders for filtration media sufficient to produce roughly 100 million face masks in 2020 (less than $10 million in revenue) and is leveraging its filtration know-how to expand into air-quality applications.  Other recent launches include low-emission transportation filtration media, fiber-based alternatives to plastic packaging, and digital-transfer media for natural textiles.

Management warned that the second quarter will be very challenging: during the 2008–09 recession Neenah’s sales fell roughly 30 percent at the trough, and current GDP forecasts suggest a similar pressure on demand.  Technical Products (filtration and labels in particular) should prove more resilient, while commercial-print papers face the greatest near-term risk if marketing spending remains depressed.  Raw-material cost relief is expected to moderate as pulp markets stay firm (driven in part by strong tissue demand) and recycled-office waste becomes scarcer.  Despite the earnings pressure expected in Q2, the company’s scenario analyses indicate that liquidity will remain ample, and additional spending levers are available if conditions deteriorate further.

Neenah remains committed to its dividend and to a disciplined, capital-efficient growth strategy, though acquisitions will be approached cautiously until conditions normalize.  Chief Financial Officer Bonnie Lind will retire on 1 October 2020 after 16 years with the company; Paul DeSantis has been named her successor.  Management emphasized that a strong balance-sheet, local supply-chain presence and ongoing innovation position Neenah to emerge from the downturn ready to capitalize on market opportunities.
