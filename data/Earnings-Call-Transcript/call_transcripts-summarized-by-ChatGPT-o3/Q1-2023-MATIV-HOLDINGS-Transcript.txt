# Mativ Holdings, Inc. – Q1 2023 Earnings Call Summary  

Mativ’s first quarter of 2023 is the first full three-month period that includes both legacy SWM and legacy Neenah operations, whereas the 2022 comparison base reflects only SWM. Management therefore discusses “comparable” results that strip out this mismatch.

Mativ reported sales of $679 million. On a constant-currency basis revenue rose 1 % organically (it fell 1 % after a 2-point currency headwind). Pricing was up 8 % year on year, more than offsetting a 7 % decline in volume/mix that stemmed largely from broad-based customer destocking and production interruptions in France.  

Adjusted EBITDA on a like-for-like basis fell 25 % to $66 million, and EBITDA margin contracted 300 basis points. Roughly $60 million of favourable pricing out-paced about $20 million of higher raw-material and energy costs, producing a net $40 million positive price-versus-cost spread; however, that benefit was more than wiped out by about $60 million of lost EBITDA tied to lower volumes, manufacturing inefficiencies and strike-related disruption.

Segment detail:
• Advanced Technical Materials (ATM) delivered 2 % organic growth, but adjusted EBITDA declined 7 % (≈$5 million) and margin slipped 90 bps. Release-liner revenue was particularly strong, up about 20 %.  
• Fiber-Based Solutions (FBS) posted a 1 % revenue decline and a 40 % ($19 million) EBITDA drop, with margin down more than 700 bps. Engineered Papers alone accounted for $15 million of the EBITDA erosion—one-third from direct production losses during French labour strikes (~$5 million) and two-thirds from indirect inefficiencies. Packaging & Specialty Paper contributed the remaining $4 million decline.  
Unallocated corporate expense fell by $1 million as merger synergies began to flow.

Merger-integration synergies are on schedule. Roughly $6 million of run-rate EBITDA savings were realised in Q1, and management still targets $25 million of incremental benefit in 2023. Early wins came chiefly from SG&A rationalisation; the emphasis is now shifting to procurement and supply-chain leverage. Separately, management has launched additional cost-reduction measures—labour, maintenance, SG&A, discretionary spend, working capital—that are expected to generate about $40 million of annualised savings, with benefits beginning in Q2. These cuts are incremental to the formal synergy programme and, according to management, require little or no cash outlay to implement.

Destocking remains the single largest commercial headwind and explains roughly two-thirds of volume pressure in the quarter. Customers indicate that inventory draw-downs should conclude during Q2, but management concedes visibility is still limited; they have therefore structured cost actions to “derisk” second-half performance should volumes stay muted. In addition, the French pension-reform strikes that materially curtailed Engineered Paper output in Q1 have largely abated and are expected to have only minimal impact in Q2.

Input costs are beginning to ease. Polypropylene prices have fallen since late 2022, European energy costs have receded, and pulp indices—up 10 % in Q1—are already trending lower and should be year-over-year favourable in H2. Specialty chemicals remain a cost risk but, overall, management believes full-year 2023 input costs could end up roughly flat versus 2022, sustaining a positive although moderating price/cost spread.

Interest expense was $26 million, and approximately 75 % of Mativ’s debt is fixed-rate. Under the company’s credit-agreement definition, net leverage at quarter-end was 4.1× EBITDA (the calculation includes pro-forma synergy adjustments). Management no longer expects to reach its earlier 2.5–3.5× leverage target by year-end 2023 given the Q1 shortfall, and deleveraging has become the Board’s foremost capital-allocation priority. They are scrutinising discretionary capex, working-capital intensity and portfolio options—including a potential sale of the Engineered Papers business—to accelerate debt reduction. The quarterly dividend remains serviceable from a cash-flow standpoint, but its future level will be evaluated in light of leverage goals.

Operating cash flow was –$21 million, reflecting normal first-quarter seasonal working-capital usage; with $19 million of capex, free cash flow was –$40 million. Initial 2023 capex plans have already been trimmed from $90 million to about $80 million, with further cuts possible, although spending for growth projects in release liners and melt-blown filtration remains protected.

Looking ahead, management projects “strong sequential quarterly EBITDA growth” beginning in Q2, underpinned by (1) completion of destocking, (2) the end of French strike disruptions, (3) ongoing synergy capture, (4) additional cost-reduction initiatives, and (5) continued easing in raw-material and energy prices. They see a viable path to ~$100 million EBITDA quarters in the second half of 2023 and expect to exit the year with improved operating traction, a stronger EBITDA run-rate, and momentum toward long-term profitability and deleveraging objectives.
