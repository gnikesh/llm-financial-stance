# 3M Company – Q1 2023 Earnings Call Summary

3M started 2023 with earnings and cash-flow strength, announced a large-scale restructuring program, and reaffirmed full-year guidance despite weak consumer end-markets and continued litigation overhangs.

In the first quarter, GAAP sales were $8.03 billion (-9% Y/Y) and adjusted sales (which exclude Russia, Aearo de-consolidation and the 2022 Food Safety divestiture) were $7.7 billion, down 9.7 percent.  Organic revenue declined 5.6 percent and, if the $300 million drag from lower disposable-respirator demand and last year’s Russia exit is excluded, organic revenue was essentially ‑2.2 percent.  Strength in automotive, electrical markets and abrasives was more than offset by pronounced weakness in consumer electronics and retail-oriented businesses.

Adjusted operating income reached $1.4 billion with a 17.9 percent margin (down 90 bp after offsetting price increases and aggressive cost control against inflation, lower volume and reduced factory utilisation).  Adjusted EPS of $1.97 beat consensus by $0.37, helped by tight discretionary spending and a smaller-than-expected currency headwind.  Adjusted free cash flow rose 24 percent to roughly $950 million, giving an 87 percent conversion rate, and inventories have been cut by about $500 million since last August.  Net debt is $12 billion, 1.5× EBITDA.

Segment performance showed the diverging demand picture:

• Safety & Industrial delivered $2.8 billion in revenue, down 6 percent organically; excluding disposable respirators and Russia it grew nearly 4 percent.  Operating margin fell 240 bp to 20.2 percent as respirator volumes normalised.

• Transportation & Electronics fell 11.3 percent organically to $1.7 billion.  Auto OEM sales rose about 6 percent in line with builds, advanced materials were up high single digits, but electronics plunged in the mid-30 percent range.  Margin declined 550 bp to 16.7 percent.

• Healthcare grew 1.4 percent organically (about 2 percent ex-Russia) to $2.0 billion.  Medical Solutions and Oral Care grew low single digits; Health Information Systems was flat and biopharma filtration declined.  Operating margin fell 300 bp to 17.9 percent, hurt by inflation, supply-chain friction and continued investment ahead of the planned spin.

• Consumer dropped 6.8 percent organically to $1.2 billion, pressured by weaker discretionary spending and retailer destocking.  Stationery and office products eked out low-single-digit growth; all other lines were down.  Margin slipped 180 bp to 15 percent.

Management reaffirmed 2023 guidance: adjusted organic revenue down 3 percent to flat (including a ~2 point drag from respirators and Russia), adjusted EPS of $8.50–$9.00, and 90–100 percent free-cash-flow conversion.  Q2 sales are expected at $7.7–$7.9 billion (-6 % to ‑8 % reported, low- to mid-single-digit organic decline) and adjusted EPS of $1.50–$1.75, including $0.25–$0.35 of restructuring charges; excluding those charges, Q2 EPS would be $1.85–$2.00.  Management anticipates sequential gains in auto builds, stable procedure volumes and continued consumer softness, with China moving from down 20 percent in Q1 to low-mid single-digit decline in Q2 and improving thereafter.

The company unveiled a far-reaching restructuring program designed to “make 3M more streamlined and competitive”:

• About 6,000 positions will be eliminated in addition to the 2,500 factory roles announced in January, bringing total reductions to roughly 10 percent of the global workforce.  
• Pre-tax restructuring charges are estimated at $700–$900 million (about half in 2023, mainly $175–$250 million in Q2) and annualised savings are targeted at the same $700–$900 million range, with roughly half of the savings flowing through 2023.  Forty percent of the benefits will come from supply-chain simplification, the remainder from corporate-centre and business-group cost reductions, including fewer management layers, cloud migration, closure of legacy facilities (e.g., a Minnesota conference centre) and a distributor-based model in ~30 smaller countries.  
• Management expects the program, together with normalising input costs and productivity initiatives, to expand steady-state operating margins by 200–300 basis points once fully implemented.

Leadership realignment supports the program: Mike Vale becomes Group President & Chief Business and Country Officer overseeing three of the four business groups; Karina Chavez heads Consumer, Chris Goralski leads Safety & Industrial, Ashish Khandpur continues running Transportation & Electronics, and Jeff Lavers focuses exclusively on Healthcare as it moves toward spin-off.

On litigation, 3M continues mediation in the Combat Arms earplug MDL and is defending or settling PFAS cases as appropriate while executing the exit from PFAS manufacturing and use by end-2025.  The total PFAS-exit cost remains estimated at $1.3–$2.3 billion, of which $800 million was accrued in Q4 2022.

Capital expenditures were $445 million in Q1, up 15 percent, largely for growth, productivity and sustainability projects.  The company returned nearly $900 million to shareholders during the quarter.

Management emphasised that 2023 is a “pivotal execution year”: short-term cost discipline, mid-term margin expansion from restructuring, and strategic investment in high-growth areas—automotive electrification, personal safety, semiconductors, home improvement and emerging climate-tech, automation and sustainable-packaging applications—are intended to leave 3M entering 2024 as a more focused, higher-margin, better-positioned enterprise.
