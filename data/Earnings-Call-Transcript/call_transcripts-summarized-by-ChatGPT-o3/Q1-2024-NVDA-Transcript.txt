# NVIDIA Q1 FY 2024 Earnings Call – Financial & Strategic Summary

NVIDIA reported first-quarter fiscal-2024 revenue of $7.19 billion, a 19 % sequential increase but a 13 % year-on-year decline, as the company emerged from last year’s gaming and professional-visualization inventory corrections while data-center demand for generative-AI capacity surged.

Data Center revenue reached a record $4.28 billion, up 18 % sequentially and 14 % year-on-year, driven by broad-based strength in NVIDIA’s Hopper- and Ampere-architecture GPUs and strong uptake of its full AI computing stack.  Cloud service providers such as Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, Oracle Cloud Infrastructure and AWS are racing to deploy H100 GPUs; consumer-internet firms like Meta have already placed H100-powered systems (e.g., Meta’s “Grand Teton” supercomputer) in production; and enterprise verticals—automotive, financial services, healthcare, telecom and others—are using accelerated computing for domain-specific large language models (e.g., Bloomberg’s 50-billion-parameter BloombergGPT).  NVIDIA shipped its new DGX H100 on-prem AI system, launched DGX Cloud through Azure, GCP and OCI, and introduced the NVIDIA AI Foundations model-foundry services (NeMo, Picasso and BioNeMo) to let enterprises create, fine-tune, align and guard-rail custom generative-AI models.  Early DGX Cloud adopters include ServiceNow, which is building proprietary LLMs for its 1,000s of enterprise customers.

Inference workloads, now scaling rapidly, prompted four new inference platforms—L4 for AI video, L40 for Omniverse/graphics, H100 NVL for very large language models, and the Grace Hopper Superchip for LLMs, recommender systems and vector databases.  Google Cloud became the first CSP to roll out the L4 platform via its new G2 instances and is integrating NVIDIA’s Triton inference server into Vertex AI and Kubernetes Engine.

Networking revenue also accelerated: high-performance InfiniBand and Ethernet (BlueField-3 DPUs, Spectrum-4 switches) are increasingly essential as customers interconnect tens of thousands of GPUs.  Quantum-2 400 Gbps InfiniBand is broadly deployed (e.g., Microsoft Azure), BlueField-3 is now in production at multiple hyperscalers, and Spectrum-4 Ethernet details will be unveiled at COMPUTEX.  Grace CPU samples are in customer hands; Bristol University just selected Grace for a supercomputer that is projected to be six times more energy-efficient than its predecessor.

Gaming revenue was $2.24 billion, up 22 % sequentially but down 38 % year-on-year.  Demand centred on GeForce RTX 40-Series GPUs for both notebooks and desktops, with RTX 4070, 4070 Ti, 4080 and 4090 fully ramped and the mainstream RTX 4060 Ti (shipping now) and RTX 4060 (July) extending Ada-Lovelace architecture to price points starting at $299.  NVIDIA reiterated that generative AI will transform game development and run-time experiences; collaboration with Microsoft is embedding RTX AI capabilities deep into Windows.

Professional Visualization revenue reached $295 million, rebounding 31 % sequentially though still 53 % below prior-year levels.  Workstation demand improved in public sector, healthcare and automotive as Ada-based GPUs began shipping.  NVIDIA Omniverse Cloud—managed on Microsoft Azure—launches in 2H 2023 and will connect Office 365 tools to industrial-metaverse workflows already being adopted by BMW Group, Lotus, GM and Jaguar Land Rover.

Automotive revenue was $296 million, up 1 % sequentially and 114 % year-on-year, led by DRIVE Orin ramps across new-energy-vehicle makers.  The forward design-win pipeline has grown to $14 billion over the next six years (vs. $11 billion a year ago).  BYD will extend Orin to its high-volume Dynasty and Ocean series from calendar-year 2024, though near-term China NEV production schedules are moderating.

Corporate gross margin rebounded to near-peak levels: GAAP gross margin was 64.6 % and non-GAAP 66.8 %.  Operating expenses fell modestly for the fourth consecutive quarter—GAAP opex down 3 % sequentially to ~$2.75 billion, non-GAAP opex down 1 % to ~$1.79 billion—as management balanced tight expense control with renewed investment for growth.  The company returned $99 million in cash dividends but repurchased no shares during the quarter, leaving $7 billion authorized for buybacks through December 2023.  Debt levels were not discussed; the balance-sheet narrative focused on inventory flows and capital-return capacity.

Cash flow, EPS and detailed balance-sheet figures were not provided during the call; reconciliations are available in the posted CFO commentary.

For the second quarter of fiscal 2024 NVIDIA guided to:

• Revenue of $11 billion ±2 % (implying ~53 % sequential growth), driven “largely” by data-center demand for generative-AI training and inference.

• GAAP gross margin of 68.6 % ±50 bp; non-GAAP gross margin of 70.0 % ±50 bp.

• GAAP operating expenses around $2.71 billion; non-GAAP operating expenses about $1.90 billion.

• Other income of roughly $90 million and a tax rate of 14 % ±1 %.

• Capital expenditures of $300-$350 million.

Management said demand visibility for data-center products now extends “a few quarters,” and NVIDIA has already “procured substantially higher supply” for the back half of the fiscal year, underpinning expectations of continued sequential growth beyond Q2.  CEO Jensen Huang framed the environment as the start of a decade-long shift in a $1 trillion installed base of CPU-centric data centers toward accelerated computing—“the iPhone moment” for generative AI—asserting NVIDIA’s full-stack platform offers the lowest total cost of ownership despite intensifying competition from start-ups, semiconductor incumbents and hyperscalers’ internal ASIC efforts.

Upcoming events include Huang’s COMPUTEX keynote on 29 May (Taipei), NVIDIA participation in the BofA Global Technology Conference (6 June), Rosenblatt’s Age of AI Summit (7 June) and the New Street Future of Transportation Conference (12 June).  Q2 earnings will be released on 23 August 2023.

In summary, NVIDIA exited Q1 with recovering gaming and professional sales, surging data-center demand tied to generative AI, record gross margins and disciplined expenses.  Management projects a step-function jump in Q2 revenue, notes sustained momentum into the second half, and continues to invest aggressively in GPUs, CPUs, networking, full-stack software and cloud-delivered services to capture what it views as a once-in-a-generation architectural reset of global computing infrastructure.
