# NVIDIA Q1 FY 2026 Earnings Call – Financial & Strategic Summary

NVIDIA reported another record quarter, posting fiscal-Q1 2026 revenue of $44 billion, a 69 percent year-on-year increase and above prior guidance despite the abrupt imposition of new U.S. export controls on its China-specific H20 GPU. Data Center sales reached $39 billion, rising 73 percent year-over-year, fuelled by the fastest product ramp in the company’s history—its Blackwell architecture—which already contributes roughly 70 percent of Data Center compute revenue. Gaming revenue hit an all-time high of $3.8 billion (up 42 percent YoY and 48 percent sequentially), Pro Visualization delivered $509 million (up 19 percent YoY, flat sequentially), and Automotive brought in $567 million (up 72 percent YoY, down 1 percent sequentially). Networking rebounded sharply to $5 billion, up 64 percent from the previous quarter, with Spectrum-X now annualising above $8 billion and first-quarter NVLink switch shipments already exceeding $1 billion.

Export restrictions weighed heavily on the quarter. Before the 9 April rule change, NVIDIA had recognised $4.6 billion of H20 revenue, but the ban prevented shipment of another $2.5 billion in confirmed Q1 orders and forced a $4.5 billion inventory and purchase-commitment write-down. Management warned that losing long-term access to a China AI-accelerator market they size at nearly $50 billion would materially disadvantage the company versus foreign competitors. For Q2 they expect roughly an $8 billion hole in revenue from the H20 prohibition, resulting in a “meaningful decrease” in China Data Center sales.

Even with that headwind, the company guided Q2 revenue to $45 billion ± 2 percent on “modest sequential growth” across all platforms, driven by an accelerating Blackwell ramp that is offsetting China weakness. GAAP and non-GAAP gross margins are projected at 71.8 percent and 72 percent, respectively, plus or minus 50 basis points—an improvement from the Q1 headline numbers of 60.5 percent GAAP and 61 percent non-GAAP that were depressed by the H20 charge. Excluding the one-off charge, Q1 non-GAAP gross margin would have been 71.3 percent, slightly above the earlier outlook. NVIDIA continues to target gross margins in the mid-70 percent range by year-end as Blackwell yields and mix improve.

GAAP operating expenses in Q1 grew 7 percent sequentially and non-GAAP opex 6 percent, reflecting higher head-count and compensation; Q2 guidance calls for GAAP opex of about $5.7 billion and non-GAAP opex of $4 billion, with full-year opex expected to rise in the mid-30 percent range. Other income is forecast at roughly $450 million in Q2, and the tax rate is expected to stay near 16.5 percent. The balance sheet remains debt-free, and NVIDIA returned a record $14.3 billion to shareholders during Q1 via buybacks and dividends.

Operationally, NVIDIA says the AI industry is entering a “reasoning” era that vastly increases inference workloads: Microsoft’s Azure OpenAI processed over 100 trillion tokens in Q1, five times last year’s tally. Grace Blackwell NVL72 racks, containing 72 Blackwell B200 GPUs, are now generally available; major hyperscalers are already each installing nearly 1,000 racks per week (about 72,000 GPUs). The company’s next step, Blackwell Ultra (GB300), began sampling in early May; with 50 percent more HBM it will boost dense FP4 inference performance another 50 percent and ships later this quarter. In MLPerf tests a single NVL72 system produced 30-times the inference throughput of an 8-GPU H200 box on Llama 3.1.

Networking demand continues to surge as customers adopt NVLink for “scale-up” compute fabrics and Spectrum-X Ethernet for “scale-out” AI clusters. Spectrum-X, which improves GPU-cluster utilisation from roughly 50 percent to as high as 90 percent via extremely low-latency, congestion-controlled Ethernet, added Google Cloud and Meta in Q1 to an install base that already included CoreWeave, Microsoft Azure, Oracle Cloud and xAI. The new NVLink Fusion programme will let ASIC, CPU and DSP partners such as MediaTek, Marvell, Alchip, Astera, Fujitsu and Qualcomm connect semi-custom accelerators directly into the NVLink ecosystem.

Beyond the cloud, NVIDIA is positioning for three new secular growth engines:

• Sovereign AI: nearly 100 NVIDIA-powered national AI factories are in flight—double last year—with average GPU counts per site also doubling. Jensen Huang cited projects in Saudi Arabia (500 MW) and the UAE (5 GW) and said many more, including one backed by the Taiwanese government, are forthcoming.  
• Enterprise AI: the new RTX Pro Enterprise AI servers, DGX Spark and DGX Station (up to 20 petaFLOPS), bring on-prem AI that integrates easily with existing IT. Major software and consulting firms (Accenture, Cisco, SAP, Deloitte, Databricks, Microsoft, Capital One, etc.) are already adopting NeMo, NIM and other microservices to tune proprietary models, cut latency and boost accuracy.  
• Industrial/Robotics AI: Omniverse, Isaac GR00T foundation models and Cosmos World simulation are enabling digital-twin factories, humanoid robots and autonomous systems for customers including TSMC, Foxconn, Pegatron, KION, Boston Dynamics, XPENG Robotics and GE Healthcare. TSMC reports Omniverse has cut fab-design timelines by months, and Foxconn cites 150-times faster thermal simulations.

Gaming remained robust—helped by the mainstream GeForce RTX 5060/Ti launch, Copilot+-capable laptops and the announcement of Nintendo Switch 2 built on a custom RTX GPU with DLSS—while the installed GeForce base now exceeds 100 million potential AI-PC users. In automotive, the Mercedes-Benz CLA will be the first in-production vehicle running NVIDIA’s full-stack platform, and the company is co-developing next-generation systems with General Motors.

Looking forward, management reiterated a one-year cadence of major GPU launches through 2028, continuous software optimisation that has historically quadrupled performance over a chip’s life, and ongoing capacity expansions across TSMC’s six fabs and two advanced-packaging plants in Arizona along with new super-computer assembly sites in Houston (Foxconn) and Fort Worth (Wistron). Despite the loss of Hopper-class sales in China, NVIDIA believes its multi-pronged strategy—training, inference, networking, sovereign, enterprise and industrial AI—positions the company for sustained, sequential growth, with CEO Jensen Huang concluding that AI “will be larger than any platform shift before, including the internet, mobile and cloud.”
