# 3M Q2 2020 Earnings Call – Financial Summary and Outlook  

3M navigated the COVID-19 downturn with solid operational execution but experienced sharp top-line and earnings pressure in the second quarter of 2020. Management emphasised strong cash generation, aggressive cost control and balance-sheet reinforcement while continuing to invest in R&D, capacity for respirators and the company’s digital/automation transformation.

Quarter-to-Date Financials  
• Net sales were $7.18 billion, down 12.2 percent year-on-year; organic revenue fell 13 percent.  
• Adjusted EPS came in at $1.78, 16.4 percent below the prior year and two cents short of the Street.  
• Adjusted operating income was $1.4 billion, yielding a 19.6 percent margin. Adjusted EBITDA margin expanded 110 basis points to 26.5 percent, helped by pricing, lower raw-material costs and $400 million of temporary spending reductions.  
• 3M booked a $58 million restructuring charge tied to pandemic-related actions; overall, restructuring and volume losses reduced operating margin roughly 100 bps versus Q2 2019.  
• Foreign-exchange translation and hedging shaved another 10 bps from margin and $0.05 from EPS, while Acelity purchase-accounting costs and related financing lowered EPS $0.07 and compressed margin 80 bps.  

Cash Flow, Leverage and Capital Allocation  
• Adjusted free cash flow surged 18 percent to $1.5 billion on tight working-capital management and a $400 million tax-payment deferral (the cash tax will reverse in Q3). H1 free cash flow totalled $2.5 billion versus $2 billion a year ago.  
• Net debt declined $1.7 billion (10 percent) sequentially; quarter-end cash and marketable securities stood at $4.5 billion.  
• Share repurchases remained suspended; 3M returned $846 million to shareholders via dividends.  
• Q2 cap-ex was $379 million; full-year spending is now forecast at roughly $1.4 billion (up $0.1 billion from the prior view) as paused projects restart.  
• Management reaffirmed its capital-allocation hierarchy: fund the business, sustain the dividend (now yielding >3%), then evaluate M&A and finally buybacks once economic visibility improves and leverage targets are met.  

Segment & Geographic Detail  

Safety & Industrial (S&I)  
• Organic sales −6%; personal-safety respirators grew double digits, but every other S&I business contracted on weak global industrial production.  
• Operating margin rose 180 bps to 23.8% on cost actions and last year’s restructuring benefits.  

Transportation & Electronics (T&E)  
• Organic sales −19%. Electronics slipped just 1% thanks to strength in semiconductors, data centres and factory automation, offset by smartphone weakness. Automotive OEM revenue plunged 44%, tracking a 45% decline in global car builds; commercial solutions fell about 30%.  
• Segment margin was 19.7%, pressured by volume deleverage despite cost cuts.  

Health Care  
• Organic sales −12%. Elective-procedure postponements hit Oral Care (-60%) and Medical Solutions (mid-single-digit decrease). Food Safety fell mid-single digits; Separation & Purification (biopharma filtration) grew mid-single digits given vaccine and therapeutic demand.  
• Operating margin slid to 16.8%, almost 10 percentage points lower; two-thirds of the erosion was volume-driven, one-third due to Acelity integration costs.  

Consumer  
• Organic sales −5%. Homecare posted high-single-digit growth, Home Improvement low-single-digit growth (Scotch-Brite, ScotchBlue, Filtrete and Meguiar’s were stand-outs). Stationery & Office and Consumer Health Care declined because of school and office closures.  
• Margin improved 250 bps to 23.2% on productivity and tight expense control.  

Regions  
• Asia-Pacific organic revenue −8% but China turned positive (+3%) after an 11% drop in Q1.  
• EMEA and Americas were down in the mid-teens each month.  
• Japan fell 12% on broad-based weakness.  

COVID-19 Response & Respirator Update  
• 3M produced 800 million respirators in H1 and expects full-year output of 2 billion units—a 3× increase versus 2019. Half of H1 production went to U.S. healthcare and FEMA.  
• Management anticipates respirator demand will contribute roughly 300–350 bps to company-wide organic growth in Q3.  
• The company is also pursuing membrane technologies for therapeutics, biopharma filtration and a rapid, low-cost COVID-19 diagnostic in partnership with MIT.  

Third-Quarter Outlook (No Full-Year Guidance)  
• With one week remaining in July, total sales were running up low single digits year-over-year—broadly across businesses and geographies—following consistent mid-teens declines through most of Q2.  
• Sequential improvement is evident in automotive, industrial and elective-procedure healthcare markets; demand for respirators remains robust.  
• Adjusted operating margin is projected between 20% and 21% in Q3, a 50–150 bp sequential increase despite some cost reinflation as volumes rise.  
• Management will continue to publish monthly sales updates given the fluid environment.  

Strategic & ESG Developments  
• 3M closed the Drug Delivery divestiture in May, sharpening its healthcare focus.  
• Its new global operating model and Enterprise Operations team are accelerating cycle times; for example, a U.S. plant converted to hand-sanitiser output in <72 hours.  
• Sustainability and PFAS stewardship remain priorities. The firm reached an interim consent order with the Alabama Department of Environmental Management related to historical PFAS discharges at Decatur; associated remediation costs are covered by existing reserves and ongoing operating/capex spend, none of which is deemed material. Trial dates for PFAS litigation now slip to 2021.  
• The company launched new inclusion, diversity and social-justice initiatives following the death of George Floyd in Minneapolis.  

Leadership Transition  
• Long-time CFO Nick Gangestad retires at July-end after 35 years; Monish Patolawala (former CFO of GE Healthcare) assumed the role on 1 July. Patolawala underlined the intent to sustain aggressive cost discipline, preserve liquidity and seize growth and productivity opportunities as the recovery unfolds.  

Taken together, 3M entered the back half of 2020 with an improving but still uncertain demand backdrop, a strengthened balance sheet, and clear levers—cost control, respirator capacity, R&D, and transformation initiatives—to protect earnings and cash flow while positioning for post-pandemic growth.
