# 3M Q2 2022 Earnings Call – Comprehensive Financial Summary  

3M reported solid second-quarter 2022 results while simultaneously unveiling two major strategic actions: a planned tax-free spin-off of its Health Care business and a new legal strategy designed to resolve the Combat Arms earplug litigation through a voluntary Chapter 11 filing by subsidiary Aearo Technologies.  Management emphasised that these moves are intended to “create more opportunity and reduce uncertainty”, without changing 3M’s long-standing capital-allocation priorities.

### Consolidated Q2 2022 performance  
• Net sales were $8.70 billion, 2.8 % lower year on year, but 1 % higher on an organic basis despite a four-point (-$340 million) foreign-exchange drag and COVID lockdowns in China.  
• Adjusted operating income reached $1.8 billion, yielding a 21.0 % margin.  Price increases, tight cost control and restructuring savings lifted underlying margin by 2.9 percentage points, fully offsetting a raw-material/logistics inflation hit of $270 million (-3.1 pts), a China-lockdown impact of 70 bps, a disposable-respirator volume decline of 40 bps and an unfavourable Brazilian tax comparison of 100 bps.  
• Adjusted EPS was $2.48, beating consensus by $0.04.  
• Adjusted free cash flow totalled $1.0 billion (68 % conversion); working-capital usage and the cash effect of US R&D tax capitalisation were headwinds.  
• Net debt stood at $13.3 billion, up roughly 4 % year to date.  
• Capital expenditure was $384 million in the quarter, $808 million year-to-date; full-year capex is still expected at $1.7–2.0 billion.

### Segment highlights  
Safety & Industrial (S&I)  
– Sales $2.9 billion; organic +0.7 %.  A $150 million YoY drop in COVID-related disposable respirators shaved 5.7 points from organic growth.  Abrasives, Electrical Markets, and Closure & Masking tapes all grew low double digits.  
– Adjusted operating income $630 million (-12 %), margin 21.5 % (-210 bps).

Transportation & Electronics (T&E)  
– Sales $2.3 billion; organic +0.5 %.  Auto OEM volumes rose low single digits against flat global builds; consumer-electronics solutions fell low single digits amid soft smartphone/tablet/TV demand.  Advanced Materials and Commercial Solutions rose mid-single digits; Transportation Safety declined high single digits.  
– Adjusted operating income $476 million (-7 %), margin 21.0 % (-80 bps), constrained by China COVID curbs and the continued partial shutdown at Zwijndrecht, Belgium.

Health Care  
– Sales $2.2 billion; organic +4.4 %.  Medical Solutions and Oral Care each grew low single digits as US elective procedures approached 90-95 % of pre-COVID levels.  Health Information Systems advanced mid-single digits; Separation & Purification grew high single digits; Food Safety was flat.  
– Operating income $494 million (-10 %), margin 22.7 % (-260 bps); adjusted EBITDA margin remained strong at nearly 30 %.

Consumer  
– Sales $1.3 billion; organic –2.5 % versus a very strong 18 % comparison.  Home Improvement was down high single digits; Consumer Health & Safety down low single digits; Stationery & Office up mid single digits; Home Care up low single digits.  
– Operating income $247 million (-15 %), margin 18.5 % (-220 bps).

### Updated 2022 outlook  
Management cited mounting FX pressure, stubborn inflation, uneven supply chains, COVID volatility and weaker consumer-electronics demand as reasons for a more cautious stance.  
• Organic-sales growth trimmed to 1.5–3.5 % (from 2–5 %).  
• Adjusted EPS lowered to $10.30–10.80 (from $10.75–11.25); about 80 % of the decrease stems from a stronger USD, now expected to subtract 4 % from full-year sales (vs 1 % previously).  
• Adjusted free-cash-flow conversion forecast remains 90–100 %.  
Third-quarter colour: ~5-point FX drag on sales; raw-material/logistics headwind of ~$225 million; disposable-respirator revenues down a further $100–200 million YoY; elective medical procedures and oral-care volumes to improve towards 95 % of pre-COVID levels; auto build forecasts still up sequentially but lower than start-of-year expectations.

### Inflation and pricing  
Year-to-date raw-material and logistics inflation has reached $480 million versus an initial full-year assumption of $350–450 million.  3M now expects $750–850 million of cost inflation for 2022 and continues to offset it with mid-single-digit price increases, assisted by restructuring savings and spending discipline.

### Strategic actions  

Health Care spin-off  
• 3M will separate its Health Care business via a tax-free spin by year-end 2023, after the Food Safety carve-out to Neogen (target close 1 September 2022).  
• 2021 Health Care revenue was $8.6 billion (including ~$400 million Food Safety).  The new entity will start with net leverage of 3–3.5 × adjusted EBITDA but is expected to delever rapidly given robust cash flow.  
• 3M will retain a 19.9 % stake, to be monetised over time, and will receive a dividend from the new company at separation.  
• Separation costs are estimated at $1.0–1.5 billion over roughly two years; stand-up “public-company” costs are pencilled in at ~$100 million for Health Care, while stranded-cost exposure at new 3M is initially c.1.5 % of revenue, with mitigation plans underway.  
• Post-spin “new 3M” will retain ~$26.8 billion of 2021 sales across Industrial, Safety, Transportation & Electronics and Consumer, supported by its technology platforms, global manufacturing, powerful brands and strong balance sheet.

Combat Arms earplug litigation  
• Subsidiary Aearo Technologies has voluntarily filed for Chapter 11 to establish a court-supervised trust to resolve the ~235,000 Combat Arms claims.  
• 3M will fund the trust with an initial $1 billion plus $240 million for projected case expenses; an independent expert (Bates White) performed the claims estimation.  Additional funding will be provided if required.  
• Aearo will indemnify 3M for related liabilities; 3M itself is not in Chapter 11 and continues normal operations.  Management believes this route will deliver faster, more equitable resolution than litigating thousands of individual cases.  
• 3M recorded a pre-tax charge of ~$1.2 billion this quarter for the planned funding.  Together with a $355 million charge tied to remediation at the Zwijndrecht PFAS site (approx. $500 million full-year impact) and ~$0.55 of other litigation expense, 2022 litigation-related charges total roughly $2.2 billion.  

PFAS and other environmental matters  
3M reiterated its commitment to 99 % reduction of PFAS discharges, ongoing site remediation, and vigorous defence of PFAS-related lawsuits; no structural or bankruptcy actions are currently planned for PFAS liabilities.

### Capital allocation and liquidity  
The dividend remains a top priority.  Share-repurchase activity is suspended until the Food Safety separation closes, but the long-standing hierarchy—organic investment, dividend, strategic M&A, and buybacks—remains intact.  Management stressed that new 3M will be well-capitalised, with strong cash flow and additional flexibility from the Health Care dividend and eventual sale of the retained 19.9 % stake.

### Operational and macro outlook  
Management sees mixed signals: improving automotive build rates in H2, strong semiconductor, data-centre and factory-automation demand, rising elective-procedure volumes, and a rebound in China after June; but ongoing COVID risks, geopolitical strains (especially in Europe), broad inflation dampening consumer demand, and soft consumer-electronics markets.  The company has contingency cost-control measures ready and will continue to emphasise pricing, productivity and working-capital efficiency to protect margins and cash flow.

### Bottom line  
3M delivered in-line-to-better Q2 results in a difficult macro environment, is sharpening its portfolio focus by creating two independent public companies, and is pro-actively addressing its largest litigation overhang.  Despite currency and inflation headwinds that have trimmed 2022 guidance, management expects to maintain strong margins, robust cash generation and an attractive dividend while positioning both new 3M and the future Health Care entity for long-term growth and value creation.
