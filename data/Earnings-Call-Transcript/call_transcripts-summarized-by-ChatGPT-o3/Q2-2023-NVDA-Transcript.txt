# NVIDIA Q2 FY 2023 Earnings Call – Financial Summary

NVIDIA reported a difficult second quarter, with results well below its May outlook, primarily because of a sharp, unexpected slowdown in consumer-facing businesses and lingering supply-chain bottlenecks. Management emphasized that the long-term demand drivers for Gaming, Data Center, Automotive and Omniverse remain intact, but near-term visibility is clouded and the company is deliberately under-shipping in Gaming and Pro Visualization to let channel inventories normalize ahead of next-generation product launches.

NVIDIA’s key Q2 financial metrics are as follows:
• Total revenue was $6.70 billion, down 19 % sequentially and up 3 % year over year, versus the company’s prior outlook of $8.10 billion.  
• GAAP gross margin declined to 43.5 % (non-GAAP 45.9 %), including $1.22 billion of inventory and related reserves and a $122 million warranty reserve.  
• GAAP operating expenses were $2.37 billion (up 36 % YoY, down 32 % QoQ because Q1 contained the $1.35 billion ARM termination charge). Non-GAAP opex rose 38 % YoY and 9 % QoQ to $1.75 billion, driven by added head-count, wage inflation and continued R&D.  
• GAAP EPS was not disclosed on the call; full reconciliation appears in the company’s filings.  
• NVIDIA returned $5.5 billion to shareholders (repurchases + dividends) in 1H FY 2023 and still has nearly $12 billion authorized through December 2023.  
• The balance sheet carries no material long-term debt; liquidity was not raised as a concern.

Segment results underscore the divergent macro environment:
Gaming: Revenue fell to $2.04 billion, down 44 % QoQ and 33 % YoY, hit by weaker European demand (war in Ukraine), COVID lockdowns in China, collapsing consumer sentiment, lower ASPs, excess channel inventories and fading crypto-mining activity. Estimated combined sell-through for Q2+Q3 is roughly $5 billion; NVIDIA is deliberately shipping “well below” that level over the next two quarters, discounting current Ampere GPUs and “price-positioning” the channel for the imminent next-generation architecture. Long-term fundamentals remain robust: GeForce sell-through is more than 70 % above pre-pandemic levels, concurrent Steam users are up a similar amount, and GeForce NOW membership exceeds 20 million.

Professional Visualization: Revenue was $496 million, down 20 % QoQ and 4 % YoY as enterprise work-station demand slowed and OEMs cut inventories. Management expects similar weakness in Q3. Nonetheless, AI and Omniverse-related workloads have meaningfully broadened the addressable market since 2020, and hybrid work is expected to keep structural demand healthy longer term.

Data Center: Revenue reached a record $3.81 billion, up 1 % QoQ and 61 % YoY but “somewhat short” of internal targets because of supply-chain kitting constraints. U.S. hyperscale and cloud sales rose sequentially, almost doubling YoY, but were offset by a pronounced slowdown at Chinese hyperscalers. Vertical-industry revenue grew sequentially and YoY. Networking (Ethernet adapters, Spectrum-4 switches, ConnectX-6/7) led segment growth. NVIDIA highlighted strong new workloads—large-language-model (LLM) training/inference, deep-learning recommendation, and autonomous-vehicle fleet processing—and said GPU rental demand in the cloud “far exceeds current supply.” Hopper-architecture H100 production has begun; substantial shipments should start in Q4, alongside the Grace CPU and Grace-Hopper Superchip launches.

Automotive: Revenue jumped to $220 million, up 59 % QoQ and 45 % YoY, marking an “inflection point” as Orin-based ADAS/AV programs ramp at NIO, Li Auto, JIDU, Human Horizons and Pony.ai. The $11 billion design-win pipeline supports continued sequential growth, and management reiterated its expectation that Automotive will become NVIDIA’s next $1-billion-plus annual business.

Inventory write-downs and margins: The $1.22 billion inventory charge primarily covers prior-generation Gaming and Data Center components procured during the supply crunch of 2021–2022 whose demand outlook has since diminished. Management said the write-down reflects both immediate Q2/Q3 needs and a longer-term recalibration; no additional write-offs are anticipated in Q3 guidance. Hopper-related inventory was not impaired, and expectations for the new generation have increased because Transformers and LLMs are scaling faster than originally forecast.

Q3 FY 2023 outlook (ending October 2022) reflects continued inventory digestion and phased launches:
• Revenue: $5.90 billion ±2 %.  
• GAAP gross margin: 62.4 % ±50 bp; non-GAAP: 65.0 % ±50 bp (no benefit assumed from selling written-down inventory).  
• GAAP opex: ~$2.59 billion; non-GAAP opex: ~$1.82 billion.  
• Other expense: ~$10 million.  
• Tax rate: ~9.5 % ±1 %.  
• Capex: $550–600 million.  
Gaming and ProViz are expected to decline further QoQ, roughly a 3:1 split in absolute dollars, while Data Center should rise modestly (similar to the Q1-to-Q2 dollar increase) and Automotive is projected to grow sequentially. Management reiterated that it is selling below market demand in Gaming to restore healthy channel inventories by year-end.

Strategic highlights and qualitative comments:
• NVIDIA’s GPUs now power 72 % of Top500 supercomputers and 22 of the 30 most energy-efficient Green500 systems.  
• Software advances have delivered 6× performance gains on A100 platforms in two years; the updated NeMo Megatron framework cuts LLM training time by up to 30 %.  
• Omniverse continues to gain traction, bolstered by a new partnership with Siemens Xcelerator and the launch of Avatar Cloud Engine and 11 additional USD connectors (total: 112).  
• Jensen Huang repeated that GeForce ASPs should trend toward premium game-console price points (~$500) as the platform’s utility broadens to content creation and cloud gaming.  
• The company has slowed the pace of opex expansion but still expects full-year non-GAAP expenses to grow “over 30 %,” balancing near-term profitability with long-term innovation.

Management concluded by acknowledging the near-term turbulence but stressed confidence that new products—Ada-generation GeForce GPUs, Hopper/Grace for Data Center, and Orin/DRIVE for Automotive—plus structural AI and metaverse adoption will restore growth as inventory normalizes, supply constraints ease and macro headwinds subside.
