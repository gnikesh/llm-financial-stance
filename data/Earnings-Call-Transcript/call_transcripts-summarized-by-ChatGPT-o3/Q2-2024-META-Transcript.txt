# Meta Platforms, Inc. – Q2 2024 Earnings Call Summary  

Meta delivered another quarter of strong top-line growth, expanding user engagement and continued investment in artificial intelligence and the metaverse, while reiterating its commitment to cost discipline and shareholder returns.

Second-quarter revenue rose 22 % year over year (23 % in constant currency) to $39.1 billion.  Family of Apps (FoA) contributed $38.7 billion of that total, up 22 %, as advertising revenue reached $38.3 billion.  Ad impressions increased 10 % and average price per ad also climbed 10 %, helped by improved AI-driven targeting and higher advertiser demand.  Online commerce remained the largest vertical contributor to growth, followed by gaming and entertainment/media.  On a geographic basis, rest-of-world ad revenue grew 33 %, Europe 26 %, Asia-Pacific 20 %, and North America 17 %.  FoA “other” revenue grew 73 % to $389 million, driven primarily by WhatsApp Business messaging fees.

Total expenses of $24.2 billion were up just 7 %, reflecting higher infrastructure and Reality Labs inventory costs that were partially offset by lower restructuring, head-count and legal expenses.  Operating income reached $14.8 billion, producing a 38 % operating margin versus 29 % a year ago.  Net income jumped to $13.5 billion and diluted EPS to $5.16.

Capital expenditures, including finance-lease payments, were $8.5 billion as Meta continued to scale data-center and network capacity for its accelerating AI road map.  Free cash flow amounted to $10.9 billion.  The company repurchased $6.3 billion of Class A shares, paid $1.3 billion in dividends, and closed the quarter with $58.1 billion in cash and marketable securities against $18.4 billion of debt.  Head-count ended the quarter at roughly 70,800, up 2 % sequentially but still about 1 % below the prior-year level.

Segment detail shows FoA expenses of $19.4 billion (about 80 % of company total) and operating income of $19.3 billion for a 50 % margin.  Reality Labs generated $353 million of revenue (+28 %), incurred $4.8 billion of expense (+21 %) and posted a $4.5 billion operating loss, consistent with Meta’s long-term mixed-reality and augmented-reality investment plan.

Daily active people across at least one Meta app reached approximately 3.27 billion in June.  Facebook’s focus on 18- to 29-year-olds is paying off, with young-adult usage in the U.S. rising for several quarters.  WhatsApp now exceeds 100 million monthly actives in the United States, and Threads is approaching 200 million global monthly actives only a year after launch.

Artificial intelligence remains the central strategic theme.  AI-based recommendation systems continue to lift engagement, particularly after Facebook’s June rollout of a unified full-screen video player that merges Reels, long-form and live video under a single ranking algorithm.  Mark Zuckerberg said Meta ultimately aims to run “a single unified recommendation system” across all content surfaces, from video to “people you may know.”

For advertisers, AI already determines audiences more accurately than manual targeting; next, it will generate and dynamically personalize creative.  Advantage+ Shopping campaigns are delivering 22 % higher return on ad spend for U.S. advertisers, and more than one million advertisers used at least one of Meta’s new generative-AI ad tools (image expansion, background generation or text generation) in the past month.  Meta Lattice, a new ad-ranking architecture, continues to improve performance, while a “flexible format” feature now lets Advantage+ automatically choose the optimal combination of images and videos to maximize conversions.  Over time, Zuckerberg expects advertisers will simply supply a business goal and budget while Meta’s systems handle audience, creative and placement end-to-end.

Meta AI, the company’s free consumer assistant, has handled “billions of queries” and is “on track to be the most-used AI assistant by year-end.”  The newly released AI Studio allows creators, businesses and eventually any user to build branded or topic-specific chat agents that can field community questions, create content or drive commerce.  In parallel, the WhatsApp Business platform—already the primary source of FoA “other” revenue—has begun alpha tests of business-grade AI agents that can recommend products, handle sales and generate leads; management sees this ultimately “dramatically accelerating” business-messaging monetization.

All of these experiences rest on the open-source Llama family of foundation models.  April’s release of Llama 3.1 includes a 405-billion-parameter “frontier-level” model and smaller variants whose cost-performance Zuckerberg argues beats leading closed systems.  He likened Llama’s open-source ecosystem to Linux, emphasizing that widespread developer use drives tooling, efficiency gains and silicon optimizations that Meta alone could not match.  Llama 4 training is already under way and is expected to require roughly 10 times the compute used for Llama 3, necessitating major near-term infrastructure expansion.  Meta prefers to build capacity ahead of demand because data-center lead times are long; nevertheless, equipment is designed with fungibility so GPUs bought for training can later be redeployed to inference or traditional ranking workloads.

AI is also accelerating Reality Labs’ product cadence.  Ray-Ban Meta smart-glasses, created with EssilorLuxottica, remain supply-constrained but are outperforming expectations as AI voice and vision features improve.  Quest 3 mixed-reality headsets are selling ahead of plan as well, and Horizon Worlds usage is climbing across VR, mobile and desktop.  Additional AI and metaverse announcements are slated for the Meta Connect conference on 25 September.

Guidance reflects continued momentum but tougher comparisons.  Management projects Q3 2024 revenue of $38.5 billion to $41.0 billion; the midpoint implies roughly 16 % year-over-year growth and includes a 2 percentage-point foreign-exchange headwind.  Full-year 2024 expense guidance is unchanged at $96 billion to $99 billion, with Reality Labs losses still expected to “increase meaningfully.”  CapEx guidance rises to $37 billion–$40 billion for 2024 (from $35 billion–$40 billion) and is now expected to grow “significantly” again in 2025 to support ever-larger AI models.  The full-year tax rate should land in the mid-teens.  While Meta is not yet offering quantitative 2025 guidance, management cautioned that depreciation and operating costs tied to the expanding infrastructure footprint will be “a significant driver of expense growth” next year.

Overall, Meta’s core advertising franchise continues to compound at double-digit rates, benefiting from AI-driven engagement gains, rising ad prices and expanding video inventory.  Robust free cash flow is funding accelerated AI and metaverse investment, large-scale share buybacks and a growing dividend, while the balance sheet remains net-cash-positive.  Management believes the company’s combination of scale, open-source leadership, hardware integration and advertising expertise uniquely positions it to monetize the next wave of AI-enabled consumer and business experiences over the coming decade.
