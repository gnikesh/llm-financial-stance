# NVIDIA Corporation – Q2 FY 2024 Earnings Call Summary  

NVIDIA reported record Q2 FY 2024 revenue of $13.51 billion, growing 88 % sequentially and 101 % year on year, and surpassing its prior outlook of $11 billion. GAAP gross margin expanded to 70.1 % (non-GAAP 71.2 %), reflecting a richer mix of high-end data-center products and the growing software component embedded in those systems. GAAP operating expenses rose 6 % sequentially to about $2.71 billion, and the company returned $3.4 billion to shareholders through buy-backs and dividends; the board simultaneously approved a new $25 billion share-repurchase authorization, leaving $29 billion available in total.  

Data Center revenue reached an all-time high of $10.32 billion, up 141 % versus Q1 and 171 % versus the prior year. Compute revenue within this segment nearly tripled, driven by surging orders from cloud service providers (CSPs) and large consumer-internet companies for HGX systems based on the Hopper (H100) and Ampere (A100) GPUs. Networking revenue, anchored by Mellanox InfiniBand, almost doubled year on year. CSPs accounted for “a little more than 50 %” of Q2 Data Center sales, followed by consumer-internet firms, with enterprise and HPC customers making up the balance. NVIDIA said demand in China remained within its long-standing 20 %–25 % contribution range to Data Center revenue, and management does not expect prospective U.S. export restrictions on advanced GPUs to have an immediate, material impact on results, though a long-term ban would forfeit a sizeable market opportunity.  

Gaming revenue rebounded to $2.49 billion, up 11 % sequentially and 22 % year on year, helped by the RTX 40-series ramp and strong laptop demand ahead of back-to-school and holiday builds. Professional Visualization produced $375 million, up 28 % sequentially but down 24 % year on year as workstation demand normalizes. Automotive revenue was $253 million, down 15 % sequentially yet up 15 % year on year as DRIVE Orin continues to scale at new-energy vehicle makers, though broader China auto softness trimmed near-term volumes.  

Management emphasized that the industry is undergoing two simultaneous platform shifts—toward accelerated computing and toward generative AI—and estimated roughly $1 trillion of installed global data-center infrastructure will migrate from general-purpose CPUs to accelerated architectures. NVIDIA’s full-stack approach (silicon, systems, networking, software and algorithms) and its enormous installed base of CUDA-capable GPUs were cited as key competitive moats.  

Key product and ecosystem developments during the quarter included:  
• Robust ramp of H100-based HGX systems (each comprising ~35,000 parts), now in volume at AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, Meta, Oracle Cloud and a growing roster of “GPU cloud” specialists.  
• Introduction of the L40S GPU, a standard-form-factor accelerator optimized for fine-tuning generative-AI models and for broad enterprise deployment; nearly 100 server SKUs from Dell, HPE, Lenovo and others will carry L40S.  
• Launch of Spectrum-X, an Ethernet-based platform that marries Spectrum switches with BlueField-3 DPU “SuperNICs” to deliver up to 1.5× higher AI throughput versus conventional Ethernet in hyperscale settings.  
• Advances in Grace-Hopper: the GH200 superchip entered full production; a second-generation version with HBM3e memory arrives in Q2 CY 2024. DGX GH200 systems (256 Grace-Hopper nodes linked via NVLink) ship later this calendar year, with Google, Meta and Microsoft among first customers.  
• Major software and partnership announcements: VMware “Private AI Foundation with NVIDIA,” Snowflake collaboration on custom LLMs, ServiceNow/Accenture “AI Lighthouse,” Hugging Face training service on HGX cloud, and WPP/Adobe/Get­ty deals leveraging Omniverse and Picasso for generative-content workflows.  

For Q3 FY 2024, NVIDIA guided to revenue of $16.0 billion ±2 %, driven primarily by further Data Center acceleration and aided by incremental gaming and ProViz growth. Gross margin is expected at 71.5 % GAAP (72.5 % non-GAAP) ±50 bp. GAAP operating expenses should be about $2.95 billion ($2.0 billion non-GAAP), with other income near $100 million and a 14.5 % tax rate ±1 %. Management stated it has “excellent visibility” into demand well into next fiscal year and is working with suppliers to increase capacity each quarter; no quantitative percentage for the supply ramp was disclosed.  

Standalone software revenue—principally subscriptions to NVIDIA AI Enterprise—has reached an annualized “hundreds of millions of dollars” and is growing rapidly, already contributing positively to margin expansion.  

CEO Jensen Huang closed by reiterating that accelerated computing and generative AI constitute a generational, long-duration shift, contending that diverting data-center capex from conventional CPUs toward NVIDIA’s GPU-based stack yields orders-of-magnitude gains in performance, energy efficiency and total cost of ownership.
