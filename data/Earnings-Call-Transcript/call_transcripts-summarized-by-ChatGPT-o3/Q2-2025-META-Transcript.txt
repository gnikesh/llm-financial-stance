# Meta Platforms, Inc. – Q2 2025 Earnings Call Summary  

Meta delivered another quarter of strong top-line growth, robust profitability and accelerating investment in artificial intelligence, while reiterating that near-term financial performance is being managed deliberately to fund an aggressive long-term push toward “personal super-intelligence”.

During the June-quarter Meta generated total revenue of $47.5 billion, a 22 percent increase year-over-year on both a reported and constant-currency basis. Net income grew to $18.3 billion, and diluted earnings-per-share reached $7.14. Operating income was $20.4 billion, representing a 43 percent operating margin, even after total expenses rose 12 percent to $27.1 billion. Free cash flow was $8.5 billion. The company repurchased $9.8 billion of Class A stock and paid $1.3 billion in dividends, while also deploying $15.1 billion into non-marketable equity investments (including a minority stake in Scale AI). Meta ended the quarter with $47.1 billion in cash and marketable securities against $28.8 billion of debt.

Capital expenditures, including finance-lease principal payments, were exceptionally high at $17 billion as the company built data-center capacity for AI. Headcount stood at 75,900, down 1 percent sequentially after earlier performance-related reductions, but Meta expects hiring to accelerate in AI, monetization, infrastructure, Reality Labs, regulation and compliance throughout 2025-26.

Family of Apps (FoA) remains the profit engine. Segment revenue was $47.1 billion, up 22 percent, of which advertising contributed $46.6 billion (up 21 percent, or 22 percent in constant currency). Online commerce was the largest vertical driver. Ad impressions grew 11 percent and average price per ad rose 9 percent, propelled by AI-driven retrieval and ranking upgrades (“Andromeda”, “GEM”, and “Lattice”) that raised ad conversions roughly 5 percent on Instagram and 3 percent on Facebook. FoA other revenue (largely WhatsApp paid messaging and Meta Verified) grew 50 percent to $583 million. FoA expenses were $22.2 billion and operating income was $25 billion, yielding a 53 percent margin.

Reality Labs revenue edged up 5 percent to $370 million thanks to Ray-Ban Meta smart-glasses, but lower Quest sales kept the segment deeply loss-making: expenses were $4.9 billion and the operating loss was $4.5 billion.

Meta’s ecosystem continues to widen. More than 3.4 billion people used at least one Meta app daily in June. AI-enhanced recommendation systems lifted time spent 5 percent on Facebook and 6 percent on Instagram just in the quarter, and Instagram global video time rose over 20 percent year-on-year; Facebook U.S. video engagement showed a similar 20 percent gain. Meta AI, already serving over one billion monthly active users, is being upgraded iteratively to Llama 4.1 and 4.2 and, ultimately, to next-generation frontier models being developed inside the newly formed Meta Superintelligence Labs under Alexandr Wang. The company is assembling “multi-gigawatt” compute complexes—Prometheus (the first 1 GW-plus cluster), Hyperion (scalable to 5 GW) and additional “titan” clusters—to support these efforts.

Management highlighted five commercial AI opportunities: better advertising, richer user experiences, business messaging, the stand-alone Meta AI agent and AI-enabled devices. AI has already driven a 5 percent increase in time spent on Instagram and raised Ray-Ban Meta demand faster than supply; an Oakley Meta HSTN sports model will add higher-resolution cameras and longer battery life. In messaging, U.S. click-to-message revenue grew more than 40 percent, and Meta is rolling out ads in Threads feeds plus Status and Channels ads within WhatsApp. However, WhatsApp ads are expected to earn lower CPMs and remain immaterial to revenue for several years.

Looking ahead, Meta guides Q3 2025 revenue to $47.5–$50.5 billion, implying a year-on-year growth deceleration in Q4 because of a tough comparison with Q4 2024. Full-year 2025 expense guidance has been tightened to $114–$118 billion, a 20–24 percent increase, while 2025 capital expenditures are now projected at $66–$72 billion—approximately $30 billion higher than 2024 at the midpoint. The 2026 outlook points to “similarly significant” CapEx dollar growth and an even faster total expense growth rate, driven primarily by depreciation on the exploding infrastructure fleet and by compensation for newly hired AI talent. Meta is exploring joint-venture financing for data-center builds but still expects to fund most CapEx internally.

Regulatory and tax environments remain fluid. A new U.S. tax law should reduce federal cash taxes, though the 2025 effective rate will exceed Q2’s unusually low 11 percent. In Europe, the Digital Markets Act continues to pressure data-usage practices; additional EU-mandated modifications to Meta’s Less Personalized Ads (LPA) model could materially hurt European ad revenue as early as H2 2025.

Open-source strategy is unchanged: Meta will continue releasing leading models while withholding especially large or safety-sensitive systems. Management reiterated conviction that AI glasses will become the dominant interface for super-intelligence, positioning the Reality Labs hardware road-map—and the associated investment outlays—as central to the long-run value proposition.

Overall, Meta’s Q2 demonstrated strong current-period profitability, substantial shareholder returns through buybacks and dividends, and an unmistakable willingness to deploy unprecedented capital and talent in pursuit of AI-driven revenue expansion and platform differentiation over the next several years.
