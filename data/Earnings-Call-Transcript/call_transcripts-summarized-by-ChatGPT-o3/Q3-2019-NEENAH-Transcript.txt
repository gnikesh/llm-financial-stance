# Neenah, Inc. – Q3 2019 Earnings Call Summary  

Neenah generated third-quarter sales of $232 million, 6 percent below the prior-year period once the effects of currency translation and the late-2018 Vermont mill divestiture are removed.  Although the top line contracted, operating execution, lower input costs and price discipline raised profitability: adjusted earnings per share rose 25 percent to $0.95 versus $0.76 last year, while GAAP EPS increased to $0.84 from $0.75.  Consolidated operating margin expanded by more than 250 basis points, returning to what management described as “more-normalized” levels.

Operating cash flow was a robust $33 million, $10 million higher than a year ago, enabling an additional $19–$20 million reduction in debt during the quarter.  Total debt at 30 September stood at $205 million (all pre-payable), leaving the company’s debt-to-EBITDA ratio below 2× and substantial liquidity available on existing credit facilities.  Interest expense declined to $2.8 million from $3.2 million.  The quarter’s effective tax rate was only 11 percent—versus 3 percent in Q3 2018—because a $1.2 million reserve was reversed when the statute of limitations for audit expired; this added roughly $0.07 per share to earnings.  Management continues to guide to a book tax rate in the low-20-percent range and a materially lower cash tax rate as R&D credits are utilized.

Capital spending was $5 million in Q3 and $14 million year-to-date.  Full-year 2019 outlays are now projected at $25–$30 million—below the $30–$40 million “maintenance” range cited earlier—because projects are being tightly screened for rapid payback.  The company reiterated its commitment to use free cash first to reduce leverage, while still supporting a “growing, attractive” dividend and opportunistic share repurchases.

Segment performance  

Technical Products posted revenue of $132 million, down from $142 million.  About one-third of the $10 million decline stemmed from a weaker euro; excluding currency, sales fell 5 percent.  The chief drag was carpet and tape backings, particularly in Asia, where nationalism, tariffs and the stronger U.S. dollar hurt volumes; backings accounted for more than 70 percent of the segment’s top-line drop.  Conversely, the two designated growth platforms continued to advance: digital transfer media volumes rose 7 percent, in line with the business’s long-term target, and filtration volumes increased 2 percent in constant currency.  Price increases and richer mix, combined with lower input costs, more than offset reduced throughput and higher SG&A, lifting operating income 15 percent to $9.5 million (versus an adjusted $8.3 million last year).  

Management expects the sharp Asian backings decline that began early in 2019 to “lap” as 2020 begins.  Meanwhile, R&D is producing new offerings—e.g., Texcol, which enables digital images to be transferred onto natural fibers and could double the addressable market for transfer papers.  Over 20 percent of Technical Products revenue now comes from products launched in the past five years, and the Appleton, Wisconsin plant has earned ISO 14001 certification, an important credential for global filtration customers.

Fine Paper & Packaging recorded revenue of $100 million, versus $113 million.  Roughly half of the shortfall traces to the Vermont mill sale; the remainder reflects ongoing secular pressure on commercial print, the more price-sensitive nature of premium sheets, and a distributor that “radically changed its strategic focus” away from high-end paper.  Neenah formally terminated support for that distributor in Q3, reallocating inventory to strategic partners; management anticipates an additional near-term sales drag of 1–2 percent but believes this action protects brand value and reinforces its selective-distribution strategy.  Despite lower volume, adjusted operating income increased 17 percent to $15.5 million (from $13.2 million), aided by lower pulp, higher selling prices, and further freight and SG&A efficiencies; segment operating margin is back to its historical mid-teens.  The company also idled its highest-cost fine-paper machine during the quarter; accelerated depreciation related to that shutdown was about $2 million, and the move is expected to yield annual savings of up to $1 million beginning in Q4.

Cost environment and pricing  

Pulp prices, a major input, fell in Q3, reducing raw-material costs by roughly $5 million year-on-year and marking the first quarter in which price improvements more than offset the prior period’s inflation.  Management had entered 2019 targeting recovery of at least the $10 million gap that remained between 2018’s $32 million pulp inflation and the $22 million of price increases achieved; with Q3 results, the company says it is “clearly on track” to meet that objective and expects continued margin restoration in Q4 and into 2020.  Freight, which had risen $8 million after regulatory changes in 2018, has moderated; optimization efforts have shaved costs, leaving a residual headwind of only $2–$3 million versus 2017.

Pension and other corporate items  

Pension plans remain well-funded.  Cash contributions and benefit payments totaled $3 million in Q3 and are expected to be about $15 million for the year, down from more than $20 million in 2018.  Consolidated SG&A was $23 million (versus $23.5 million), while unallocated corporate SG&A declined to $3.7 million, or $3.5 million after adjusting for non-routine items.

Outlook  

Management cautioned that global macro conditions—particularly sluggish European industry and trade-related uncertainty—make demand forecasting difficult.  Fourth-quarter revenue is normally the softest because customers work down year-end inventories; Q4 will also contain the annual filtration maintenance shutdown in Germany and the transient effect of the Fine Paper distributor change.  Nonetheless, the company enters the final quarter with a stronger margin profile, continued emphasis on cost control and capital efficiency, and a balance sheet that provides flexibility for strategic acquisitions.  The longer-term growth thesis centers on (1) transportation and advanced air filtration, (2) global expansion of digital-transfer media, and (3) premium packaging to offset secular declines in fine paper, all supported by ongoing innovation and selective distribution.  Management reiterated its goal of sustaining double-digit operating margins in both core segments, maintaining debt below 2× EBITDA, and continuing to return excess cash to shareholders while funding disciplined growth initiatives.
