# 3M Company – Q3 2020 Earnings Call Summary

3M reported a clear sequential rebound from the pandemic-driven trough of the second quarter, returning to positive organic growth, expanding cash flow, and strengthening its balance sheet, even as management emphasized that the macro-economic backdrop remains highly uncertain and therefore refrained from reinstating full-year guidance.  

During the three months ended 30 September 2020, total sales reached $8.35 billion, up 4.5 percent year on year and 16 percent higher than in the previous quarter; organic growth was slightly positive at 1 percent.  Adjusted earnings per share were $2.43, beating consensus by $0.16, although they declined $0.15 versus the prior-year quarter chiefly because of a higher effective tax rate (21.4 percent versus 19 percent a year earlier) and the absence of a $58 million real-estate gain recorded in Q3 2019.  The company generated an adjusted operating margin of 22.9 percent (down 90 basis points year on year, 70 basis points of which related to last year’s property gain), while adjusted EBITDA margin stood at a robust 29 percent, with every business group at or above 27 percent.  Price realization added 60 basis points to margin, raw-material deflation added a further 20 basis points, and foreign exchange reduced margin by 30 basis points.

Cash generation was again a highlight.  Adjusted free cash flow reached $2.2 billion in the quarter, up 13 percent year on year, taking the year-to-date figure to $4.6 billion, 19 percent above the same period of 2019.  Working-capital improvement contributed more than $330 million of cash, including a $240 million sequential reduction in inventories.  Capital expenditure was $368 million in Q3 and almost $1.1 billion year to date; management now expects full-year capex of $1.4 billion to $1.5 billion, slightly higher than the previous outlook.  Strong cash flow enabled net debt to be reduced by $1.3 billion sequentially and by $2.8 billion since January; net leverage has fallen to about 1.8× EBITDA.  3M held $4.6 billion of cash and marketable securities at the end of the quarter and paid shareholders $847 million in dividends during Q3 ($2.5 billion year to date).  Share repurchases remain suspended while management “maintains financial flexibility”.

Segment results underpinned the overall recovery.  In Safety & Industrial, organic sales rose 6.9 percent and operating margin jumped 430 basis points to 27.2 percent, propelled by continued extraordinary demand for N95 respirators as well as strength in the Roofing Granules and Automotive Aftermarket businesses; abrasives, adhesives, tapes and electrical markets improved sequentially, yet stayed negative year on year.  Transportation & Electronics posted a 7.1 percent organic decline, with a 23.9 percent margin: electronics-related sales edged up 1 percent on semiconductor and data-center demand, but auto OEM sales fell 4 percent (still outperforming global builds, down 3 percent) and markets tied to hospitality, advertising and oil & gas remained weak.  Health Care delivered 8.1 percent organic growth; the 23.5 percent margin was 320 basis points lower than a year ago largely because Acelity purchase-accounting amortization and COVID-19-related spending outweighed the volume rebound.  Medical Solutions grew by the mid-teens thanks to respirators, although underlying elective-procedure exposure remained below pre-COVID levels; Oral Care returned to low-single-digit growth as dental offices reopened, and Separation & Purification (biopharma filtration) increased by the low teens.  Finally, Consumer grew organically by 5.5 percent, led by low-double-digit gains in Home Improvement and Home Care (Filtrete filters, Scotch-Brite cleaning and Command hooks); operating margin improved 200 basis points to 25.3 percent, although stationery and office products fell double-digits with schools and offices still partly closed.

Regionally, the Americas led with 5 percent organic growth (the United States low-teens in several consumer and safety categories), EMEA was flat after a 15 percent contraction in Q2, and Asia-Pacific declined 3 percent; China, however, grew 8 percent across all business groups, while Japan remained under pressure.

Management continues to fight the pandemic “from every angle”.  By late October 3M had shipped 1.4 billion respirators year to date and is on track for two billion by year-end; it will exit 2020 at an annualized capacity of roughly 2.4 billion units after adding new high-efficiency production lines, including two in Sheboygan Falls, Wisconsin that are already running 30 percent above legacy line rates.  The company is also collaborating with MIT on a low-cost, saliva-based, paper antigen test for COVID-19; prototypes have shown promising sensitivity, external validation is underway, and 3M expects to seek Emergency Use Authorization in the first half of 2021, envisaging mass-manufacturable volumes.  Beyond personal safety, 3M is channeling investment toward secular growth themes—home air-quality, e-commerce logistics, automotive electrification and biopharma filtration—while deemphasizing slower markets such as office, hospitality and oil & gas.

Looking to the fourth quarter, management anticipates that personal safety, home improvement, cleaning, semiconductors, data centers and biopharma filtration will remain strong, whereas elective medical and dental procedures, auto OEM, general industrial, consumer electronics, hospitality and office supplies are unlikely to recover to pre-COVID levels before well into 2021.  October company-wide sales are tracking flat to slightly up year on year despite one fewer trading day; respirator demand alone should add roughly three percentage points to Q4 top-line growth, though the May divestiture of the drug-delivery business will subtract about $100 million (130 basis points).  With two fewer selling days than Q3, 3M forecasts an adjusted operating margin of about 21 percent for Q4, while reiterating its priorities of sustaining strong free cash flow, disciplined capital allocation and further deleveraging.  Because of persistent economic volatility, the firm will continue to publish monthly sales data and has not reinstated full-year guidance or its share-repurchase program, but it remains confident in its ability to “lead in the economic recovery” and to capitalize on emerging demand trends.
