# NVIDIA Q3 FY 2022 Earnings Call – Financial and Strategic Highlights

NVIDIA reported another record quarter, driven by broad-based demand for its Ampere-generation GPUs and expanding software platforms.

Quarterly Performance (GAAP unless noted otherwise)  
• Revenue reached $7.10 billion, +50 % YoY and +9 % QoQ, the highest in company history.  
• Gross margin rose 260 bp YoY and 40 bp QoQ to about 65.2 % (non-GAAP 67 %).  
• Operating cash flow was $1.5 billion (up from $1.3 billion YoY but down from $2.7 billion QoQ after a $1.6 billion pre-payment for long-term supply).  
• GAAP EPS was $0.97, +83 % YoY; non-GAAP EPS was $1.17, +60 % YoY (both adjusted for the July 4-for-1 stock split).

Segment Results  
• Gaming: $3.22 billion (+42 % YoY, +5 % QoQ). Channel inventories remain lean despite increased desktop GPU supply; laptop GPUs posted strong high-end growth. Roughly a quarter of the GeForce installed base has upgraded to RTX. Nearly all Ampere desktop shipments were “LHR” (lite-hash-rate) to steer supply to gamers.  
• Data Center: $2.94 billion (+55 % YoY, +24 % QoQ). Records in both hyperscale and vertical-industry customers; hyperscale compute revenue doubled YoY on NLP and recommendation workloads. Over 25,000 companies now run NVIDIA AI; Triton Inference Server, AI Enterprise for VMware vSphere, and the global LaunchPad program are accelerating enterprise adoption.  
• Professional Visualization: $577 million (+144 % YoY, +11 % QoQ). Mobile and desktop workstations grew as hybrid work demand continued. Omniverse Enterprise entered general availability; >700 enterprises and 70,000 individual creators have evaluated the platform.  
• Automotive: $135 million (+8 % YoY, –11 % QoQ) as cockpit revenue was constrained by OEM component shortages. New DRIVE Orin design wins include Lotus, Kodiak Robotics, QCraft and WM Motor.  
• OEM & Other: Included $105 million of Crypto Mining Processor (CMP) sales; virtually all CMP revenue is expected to disappear in Q4.

Balance Sheet & Supply Commitments  
• NVIDIA has locked in multi-year capacity: $3.4 billion of long-term supply agreements, with $1.6 billion paid during Q3. Management expects improved chip and substrate availability from 2H FY 2022 onward.  

Q4 FY 2022 Outlook (non-GAAP figures in parentheses)  
• Revenue: $7.4 billion ± 2 %, driven by further growth in Data Center and Gaming, partly offset by CMP falling to “negligible” levels.  
• Gross margin: 65.3 % (67.0 %) ± 50 bp.  
• Operating expenses: $2.02 billion ($1.43 billion).  
• Other expense: ~$60 million.  
• Tax rate: 11 % ± 1 %.  
• Capital expenditures: $250–275 million.

Strategic & Product Highlights  
• Omniverse: Now shipping in enterprise form; Jensen Huang outlined a subscription model of roughly $1,000 per “creator/designer” or per digital agent/robot per year, positioning Omniverse for potentially hundreds of millions of paid seats across designers, autonomous vehicles, retail avatars and more.  
• New software stacks unveiled at GTC include Omniverse Replicator, Omniverse Avatar, Riva Enterprise conversational AI, NeMo Megatron for large-language models, BlueField DOCA 1.2 for zero-trust security, and Clara Holoscan for medical devices. CUDA downloads surpassed 30 million; the developer ecosystem nears 3 million.  
• Networking: Demand outstripped supply; company launched 400 Gb/s Quantum-2 InfiniBand platform (Quantum-2 switch, ConnectX-7 NIC, BlueField-3 DPU).  
• Robotics & Edge: Announced Jetson AGX Orin (200 TOPS) for autonomous machines, shipping 1Q CY 2022.  
• Climate & Science: “Earth-2” AI supercomputer initiative announced to enable billion-X climate modeling speed-ups.  
• Arm acquisition: Under extended scrutiny. FTC has expressed concerns; EU and UK have moved to Phase 2; Chinese SAMR review pending formal case. NVIDIA continues to argue the deal will enhance competition.  

Margin Dynamics & Capital Return  
• Datacenter mix and high-end RTX products remain the primary gross-margin tailwinds; management foresees software sold separately (e.g., Omniverse, AI Enterprise) as an additional lever for future GM expansion.  
• Share-based capital-return commentary was not provided; focus remains on growth investment and securing supply.

Management Commentary  
• Secular drivers—AI training & inference, accelerated computing, cloud gaming, creator workflows, digital twins, autonomous driving and robotics—are compounding.  
• Less than 10 % of the world’s servers are GPU-accelerated today; NVIDIA expects “every single server will be accelerated some day,” implying a long runway for Data Center growth.  
• Supply-chain actions and rising long-term commitments position the company to meet strong demand through FY 2023 and beyond.

NVIDIA closed the call projecting continued momentum in both hardware and software, with Data Center leading sequential growth, Gaming constrained only by supply, and new platforms such as Omniverse, AI Enterprise and automotive DRIVE poised to open additional multibillion-dollar markets.
