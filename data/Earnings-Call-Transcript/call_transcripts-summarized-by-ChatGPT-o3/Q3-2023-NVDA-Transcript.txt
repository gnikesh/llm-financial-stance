# NVIDIA Fiscal 3Q 2023 Earnings Call – Financial and Operating Summary

NVIDIA reported fiscal third-quarter 2023 revenue of $5.93 billion, a decline of 12 percent sequentially and 17 percent year on year.  The company faced weaker gaming demand and inventory corrections, but delivered record data-center and automotive revenue, kept operating expenses essentially flat, and reiterated that accelerated computing and AI remain its long-term growth engines.

Data-center sales reached an all-time high of $3.83 billion, up 1 percent sequentially and 31 percent from a year earlier, fuelled by continued adoption of NVIDIA AI by U.S. cloud providers, consumer internet firms, and vertical-industry customers in automotive and energy.  Gaming revenue fell to $1.57 billion, down 23 percent quarter on quarter and 51 percent year on year as the company deliberately reduced partner sell-in to rebalance channel inventory; management believes inventories will normalise by the end of 4Q.  Professional Visualization revenue dropped to $200 million, down 60 percent sequentially and 65 percent year on year for the same inventory-correction reasons.  Automotive revenue rose to a record $251 million, up 14 percent sequentially and 86 percent year on year, driven by production ramps of DRIVE Orin design wins.

GAAP gross margin was 53.6 percent and non-GAAP gross margin was 56.1 percent.  Results incorporate a $702 million inventory charge, largely for A100 data-center products affected by softer demand in China and new U.S. export controls, partially offset by a ~$70 million warranty benefit.  GAAP operating expenses grew 31 percent year on year to $2.57 billion; non-GAAP opex rose 30 percent to $1.82 billion, reflecting head-count additions, salary increases, and higher data-center infrastructure costs.  Sequential opex growth was in the single-digit percent range, and management intends to hold it roughly flat for the next several quarters.

The company generated $3.75 billion of shareholder returns during the quarter via share repurchases and dividends, leaving $8.3 billion remaining under the current buy-back authorisation (through December 2023).  No changes to debt levels were discussed, and balance-sheet leverage was not a focus of the call.

Fourth-quarter fiscal 2023 guidance calls for revenue of $6.0 billion ± 2 percent.  GAAP gross margin is projected at 63.2 percent ± 50 bp and non-GAAP gross margin at 66 percent ± 50 bp, reflecting the absence of the large inventory charge.  GAAP opex should be about $2.56 billion and non-GAAP opex about $1.78 billion.  Both GAAP and non-GAAP tax rates are expected near 9 percent.  Capital spending is forecast between $500 million and $550 million.  Management anticipates modest sequential revenue growth led by automotive, gaming (as new Ada-architecture GPUs ramp), and data center (early H100 shipments), partially offset by persisting softness in China.  Pro Visualization is expected to remain roughly flat.

Operationally, NVIDIA began shipping its Hopper-based H100 GPUs in the quarter and highlighted their 3× lower total cost of ownership and 3.5× lower energy use for equivalent AI performance versus prior generations.  Major server vendors (Dell, HPE, Lenovo, Supermicro) will ship H100 systems this quarter, and leading clouds (AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, Oracle Cloud) will launch H100 instances in early calendar 2023.  NVIDIA also announced multiyear collaborations with Microsoft and Oracle to expand cloud-AI infrastructure using tens of thousands of A100 and H100 GPUs paired with 400 Gbps InfiniBand networking and NVIDIA AI Enterprise software.  Networking revenue continued to grow strongly as supply constraints eased; Quantum-2 InfiniBand and Spectrum Ethernet are gaining momentum, and VMware’s vSphere now supports NVIDIA BlueField DPUs.

In gaming, the Ada Lovelace-based GeForce RTX 4090 launched in mid-October to strong sell-out demand, while the RTX 4080 became available on the day of the call.  Despite lower channel sell-in, management cited solid sell-through in the Americas and EMEA and expects seasonal demand plus Ada adoption to lift Q4 gaming revenue sequentially.

Professional Visualization remains pressured by weak macro conditions and ongoing inventory correction.  Nevertheless, NVIDIA is expanding Omniverse-as-a-Service and secured new enterprise uses by Lowe’s, Charter Communications, HEAVY.AI, and Deutsche Bahn for digital-twin applications.

Automotive momentum continues, with Volvo’s new EX90, Hozon Auto, NIO, Polestar, XPeng, and Zeekr adopting DRIVE Orin and, from 2025 models onward, the next-generation DRIVE Thor SoC that delivers up to 2,000 TFLOPS and can handle both autonomous-driving and infotainment workloads.

Management reiterated that structural trends—general-purpose CPU scaling limits, rising demand for energy-efficient accelerated computing, and rapid advances in AI (large language models, deep recommender systems, generative AI)—support long-term growth.  NVIDIA is adapting to near-term macro headwinds by controlling expenses, converting certain China data-center demand to compliant A800 products, and accelerating the ramp of new architectures (Ada, Hopper, Grace, Thor).

NVIDIA will report fiscal-year-end results on 22 February 2023 and will meet investors at events hosted by Credit Suisse (30 Nov), Arete (5 Dec), and JPMorgan (5 Jan).
