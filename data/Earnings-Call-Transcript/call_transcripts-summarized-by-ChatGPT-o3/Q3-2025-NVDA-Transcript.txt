# NVIDIA Fiscal 3Q25 Earnings Call – Financial and Strategic Highlights

NVIDIA reported another set of record results and laid out an aggressive ramp of its new Blackwell architecture while addressing supply constraints, margin dynamics, and the evolving AI landscape.

NVIDIA delivered fiscal 3Q25 revenue of **$35.1 billion**, up **17 % sequentially** and **94 % year-on-year**, exceeding its outlook of $32.5 billion. Non-GAAP gross margin was **75.0 %** (GAAP 74.6 %), down modestly QoQ on a mix shift toward more complex H100 systems. Non-GAAP operating expenses were up 9 % QoQ to **$3.1 billion**, reflecting higher compute, infrastructure, and R&D for next-generation products. Net income and EPS were not quoted on the call excerpt, but cash returned to shareholders totaled **$11.2 billion** (share repurchases + dividends) during the quarter.

### Segment performance
• **Data Center** set another record at **$30.8 billion** (≈88 % of revenue), **+17 % QoQ / +112 % YoY**.  
 – Cloud service providers (CSPs) represented roughly half of segment sales, more than doubling YoY.  
 – H200 (Hopper) sales reached “double-digit billions,” marking the fastest product ramp in company history.  
 – Regional CSP/cloud revenue likewise doubled YoY as North America, EMEA, and APAC scaled sovereign and regional AI clouds.  
 – Consumer Internet revenue also more than doubled YoY.  
 – Blackwell (B-series) is now in full production; 13 000 GPU engineering samples shipped in Q3 including DGX systems for OpenAI. Demand is “staggering,” and Oracle announced a 131 000-GPU zettascale cluster while Microsoft, AWS, Google Cloud, and OCI prepare instance launches.  
 – Networking revenue grew **20 % YoY** but slipped sequentially; management expects a rebound in 4Q. Spectrum-X Ethernet revenue more than tripled YoY, with multiple large deployments pending.

• **Gaming** revenue was **$3.3 billion**, **+14 % QoQ / +15 % YoY**. Notebook, desktop, and console all grew. Channel inventory is healthy but 4Q gaming revenue is expected to decline sequentially due to supply constraints as the company prioritises data-center parts. RTX AI PCs with up to 321 TOPS and Copilot+ support begin ramping in 4Q.

• **Professional Visualisation (ProViz)** delivered **$486 million**, **+7 % QoQ / +17 % YoY**, bolstered by workstation demand for AI content creation, AV simulation, and productivity AI.

• **Automotive** hit a record **$449 million**, **+30 % QoQ / +72 % YoY**, led by NVIDIA Orin ADAS/AD launches such as Volvo’s fully electric SUV.

### Geographic & regulatory notes
China data-center revenue rose sequentially on export-compliant products but remains “well below” pre-control levels. Competition there is expected to stay intense. NVIDIA reiterated strict compliance with U.S. export controls and will adapt to any future policy from the incoming U.S. administration.

### Cash, capital, and shareholder returns
NVIDIA’s software, services, and support revenue is annualising at **$1.5 billion**, expected to exit FY25 above **$2 billion**. Annualised NVIDIA AI Enterprise revenue will more than double YoY. The company repurchased shares and paid dividends totaling $11.2 billion in Q3.

### 4Q FY25 outlook (quarter ending January 2025)
• Revenue: **$37.5 billion ± 2 %** (implies ~7 % QoQ growth).  
• Blackwell revenue will exceed the prior “several-billion-dollar” estimate; Hopper demand continues.  
• GAAP gross margin **73.0 % ± 50 bps**, non-GAAP **73.5 % ± 50 bps**; margin temporarily pressured by early Blackwell mix.  
• GAAP/Non-GAAP operating expenses: **$4.8 billion / $3.4 billion** respectively.  
• Other income: **≈ $400 million**.  
• Tax rate: **16.5 % ± 1 %**.  
Management expects gross margin to dip to the “low-70s” during the initial Blackwell ramp, then recover to the **mid-70 %** range once the platform is fully ramped (potentially in the back half of calendar 2025). 

### Key strategic and qualitative takeaways
• Three scaling vectors—foundation-model pre-training, post-training (RLHF/RLAIF and synthetic data), and inference-time scaling (e.g., OpenAI’s “o1/Strawberry”)—are simultaneously driving compute demand.  
• Hopper remains supply-constrained, yet Blackwell shipments will grow each successive quarter and are expected to surpass Hopper volumes during FY26.  
• Supply chain complexity is unprecedented: seven custom chips per Blackwell system shipped in air- or liquid-cooled variants, multiple NVLink configurations, x86 or Grace CPUs, InfiniBand or Spectrum-X Ethernet. NVIDIA cited partners across fabrication, packaging, memory (HBM from SK Hynix and Micron), servers (Dell, HPE, Lenovo, Supermicro, Foxconn, Quanta, Wiwynn), and CSPs (Microsoft, AWS, Google, Oracle, CoreWeave, SoftBank, T-Mobile).  
• Sovereign AI demand remains in “low-double-digit billions” of dollars, with notable projects in India, Japan, Europe, and the Middle East.  
• Enterprise AI adoption is “full throttle”; nearly 1 000 companies are trialling NVIDIA NIM microservices, and NVIDIA AI Enterprise revenue will more than double this year.  
• Physical/industrial AI and robotics are accelerating via NVIDIA Omniverse and foundation models that understand the physical world; customers include Foxconn, SoftBank, and major auto OEMs.  
• Management sees no near-term “digestion” phase: a trillion-dollar installed base of CPU-centric data-centers must be modernised for machine learning, and new AI-native workloads will continue to emerge.  
• NVIDIA reiterated its annual GPU roadmap cadence; the company is on track for Blackwell in 2024, Rubin in 2025/26, and subsequent updates, emphasising performance-per-watt gains to maximise data-center revenue per megawatt for customers.
