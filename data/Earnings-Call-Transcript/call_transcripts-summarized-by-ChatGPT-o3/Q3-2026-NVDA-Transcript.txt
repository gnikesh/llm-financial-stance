# NVIDIA Q3 FY 2026 Earnings Call – Financial & Strategic Summary

NVIDIA delivered another record quarter and raised its near-term outlook, underscoring sustained, broad-based demand for accelerated computing and AI infrastructure.

Revenue, Profitability and Balance Sheet  
NVIDIA posted Q3 FY 2026 revenue of **$57 billion**, up **62 % year-on-year** and **22 % sequentially** (a $10 billion jump). GAAP gross margin reached **73.4 %** and non-GAAP gross margin **73.6 %**, both above guidance as favorable data-center mix, better cycle times and cost reductions offset input-price pressure. GAAP operating expenses rose **8 % QoQ**; non-GAAP opex increased **11 %**, driven by higher compute infrastructure, compensation and R&D. The tax rate ran just over **17 %**, modestly above the 16.5 % guide owing to stronger U.S. revenue.  

Inventories grew **32 % QoQ**, and purchase commitments jumped **63 %** as the company prepares for further growth. Management reiterated its intent to keep a strong balance sheet, projecting it could generate roughly half-a-trillion dollars of free cash flow over the next couple of years. Excess cash will continue to fund supply-chain resiliency, strategic equity stakes in key AI partners, and ongoing share repurchases.

Segment Performance  
• Data-Center revenue hit an all-time high of **$51.2 billion**, up **66 % YoY**. Compute grew **56 % YoY** on the GB300 (Blackwell) ramp, while networking revenue soared **162 % YoY** to **$8.2 billion** as NVLink, InfiniBand Quantum-X and Spectrum-X Ethernet deployments broadened.  
• Gaming revenue was **$4.3 billion**, up **30 % YoY** on healthy demand and normal channel inventories heading into the holidays.  
• Professional Visualization delivered **$760 million**, up **56 % YoY**, led by DGX Spark (small-form-factor Grace Blackwell systems).  
• Automotive revenue reached **$592 million**, up **32 % YoY**, reflecting expansion of Level-4 autonomy programs such as Uber’s robotaxi fleet on NVIDIA Hyperion.  

Key Financial Outlook  
For Q4 FY 2026 NVIDIA expects:  
– Revenue of **$65 billion ± 2 %** (14 % sequential growth at the midpoint, driven mainly by Blackwell momentum).  
– GAAP and non-GAAP gross margins of **74.8 %** and **75.0 %**, respectively, ± 50 bps.  
– GAAP opex of **$6.7 billion** and non-GAAP opex of **$5.0 billion**.  
– Other income of about **$500 million** and a **17 %** tax rate ± 1 %.  
Management aims to “hold gross margin in the mid-70 % range” through FY 2027 despite rising component costs.

AI Infrastructure Demand & Order Visibility  
• NVIDIA now has **visibility to roughly $500 billion in cumulative Blackwell and Rubin revenue from early 2025 through calendar-year 2026**; $51 billion shipped in Q3 alone. New agreements (e.g., Kingdom of Saudi Arabia’s 400-600 k GPU order over three years and Anthropic’s initial 1 GW Grace Blackwell/Rubin commitment) could expand that figure.  
• Management estimates hyperscaler and CSP aggregate 2026 CapEx at **~$600 billion**, up more than $200 billion since January, and sees a **$3–4 trillion annual AI-infrastructure opportunity by decade’s end**.  
• Recently announced “AI factory” projects total an unprecedented **5 million GPUs**, spanning hyperscalers, sovereign clouds, enterprise build-outs and scientific supercomputing centers.  
• Consistent with last quarter’s stance, the Q4 outlook **assumes no China data-center compute revenue** because of export restrictions and intensified local competition.

Product & Technology Road-map  
• Blackwell (GB300) already accounts for roughly **two-thirds** of Blackwell revenue and is shipping in volume to all major hyperscalers and GPU cloud providers.  
• Hopper (H100/H200) remains fully utilized, generating ~**$2 billion** of revenue in its 13th quarter.  
• Rubin (Vera Rubin platform, seven chips, third-generation rack-scale design) is on schedule to ramp in **2H 2026**, promising another “X-factor” leap in performance and cost-per-token. Early silicon is back from fabs and initial bring-up is progressing well.  
• Networking momentum accelerates: NVLink scale-up, Spectrum-X Ethernet, InfiniBand Quantum-X and the new Spectrum-XGS “scale-across” technology collectively position NVIDIA as the only supplier with scale-up, scale-out and scale-across fabrics for AI factories.  
• NVLink Fusion partnerships announced with Fujitsu (CPU–GPU integration) and Intel (multi-generation custom data-center and PC products); Arm will incorporate NVLink IP into future CPU SoCs.  
• In MLPerf, Blackwell Ultra trained models **5× faster than Hopper**, while semi-analysis’ InferenceMAX tests showed **10× better performance-per-watt and cost-per-token** versus H200 on DeepSeek-R1.  
• CUDA Dynamo, the low-latency modular inference framework, has been adopted by AWS, Azure, GCP and OCI to accelerate MoE and other complex model deployments.  

Strategic Partnerships & Equity Investments  
• NVIDIA is working with OpenAI on at least **10 GW of new AI data centers** and has the option to invest more capital; NVIDIA will continue supplying GPUs via Microsoft Azure, OCI and CoreWeave.  
• A new deep partnership with **Anthropic** brings Claude models to the NVIDIA platform for the first time; Anthropic’s initial commitment equals **~1 GW** and includes Rubin-generation systems.  
• Other strategic stakes include Mistral, Reflection, Thinking Machines Lab, Safe Superintelligence and xAI, all aimed at expanding the CUDA ecosystem.  
• AI factory collaborations include xAI’s **Colossus 2** (world’s first gigawatt-scale data center), Lilly’s drug-discovery factory, and Saudi Arabia’s HUMAIN build-out co-developed with AWS.

Supply Chain & Capacity  
NVIDIA continues to lock in long-term capacity across TSMC, advanced packaging, HBM vendors, substrate suppliers and system ODMs. The company celebrated first U.S.-produced Blackwell wafers and is broadening U.S. assembly/​test with Foxconn, Wistron, Amkor and SPIL over the next four years. Management sees power, land and financing as real yet solvable constraints, emphasizing that NVIDIA’s superior performance-per-watt and diverse demand offtake make it the preferred partner for new AI-factory operators.

Capital Allocation Philosophy  
Management stressed four priorities:  
1. Maintain a fortress balance sheet to guarantee supply continuity and give partners confidence in NVIDIA’s purchase commitments.  
2. Reinvest aggressively in R&D (CPU, GPU, networking, software and full-stack systems) to preserve annual “X-factor” performance leaps and expand TCO leadership.  
3. Continue substantial share repurchases.  
4. Deploy minority equity investments only when they broaden CUDA’s reach, deepen co-development ties and promise outsized financial returns.

Concluding View  
CEO Jensen Huang dismissed talk of an “AI bubble,” citing three simultaneous platform shifts—CPU-to-GPU accelerated computing, classical ML-to-generative AI, and generative-to-agentic/physical AI—that he believes will support multiyear infrastructure expansion across hyperscalers, enterprises, sovereign clouds and emerging industries. NVIDIA’s singular architecture, full-stack expertise and unmatched ecosystem penetration position it to capture a significant share of what management sees as a multi-trillion-dollar opportunity.
