# Schweitzer-Mauduit International (SWM) – Q4 & FY-2021 Earnings Call Summary

Schweitzer-Mauduit International closed a turbulent 2021 with record top-line expansion, but inflation, supply-chain friction and raw-material spikes weighed heavily on margins, earnings and cash generation. Management nevertheless described the year as strategically successful—highlighted by the Scapa acquisition—and projected a pronounced rebound in 2022 driven by firmer pricing, recovering cost spreads, stronger free cash flow and full-year contributions from acquired assets.

Management spoke at length about three central topics: (1) the company’s belated, but now largely completed, repricing response to raw-material inflation; (2) the strategic benefits and still-untapped synergy potential of Scapa; and (3) a 2022 outlook that calls for double-digit sales growth, 20–30 % EBITDA expansion, significantly higher EPS and measurable deleveraging.

## 2021 Consolidated Performance

• Net sales rose 34 % to $1.44 billion; organic sales increased 4 %.  
• Adjusted operating profit fell 7 % to $159 million as cost inflation more than offset volume growth.  
• GAAP EPS improved to $2.80 (from $2.66), but adjusted EPS slipped to $3.10 (from $3.68).  
• Polypropylene and wood-pulp inflation that was not recovered in pricing cut EPS by an estimated $0.55, while lost transportation-film volume (linked to specialty TPU shortages) removed another ~$0.30.  
• Operating cash flow came in at $58 million; after $39 million of capex, free cash flow was only $19 million. Acquisition fees ($19 million) and a $56 million working-capital build depressed cash generation temporarily.  
• Net debt finished “just under” $1.2 billion; leverage under the credit agreement stood at 4.8× adjusted EBITDA. Liquidity was $177 million (cash $75 million plus $102 million of revolver availability). Proceeds of ~$35 million from the sale of the Spotswood site were applied to the revolver.

## Advanced Materials & Structures (AMS)

• Segment sales surged 71 % on the Scapa acquisition and 11 % organic growth. Scapa contributed $306 million for the nine months owned.  
• Adjusted operating profit increased 17 %, but margin contracted 530 bp to 11.5 % (Q4 margin 7.3 % vs 17.5 % a year earlier) because resin inflation (> $30 million hit) and higher freight/labor costs out-ran pricing.  
• Filtration revenue grew ~25 %; transportation grew ~21 % despite “tens of millions of dollars” of unfilled orders owing to TPU resin scarcity. Construction and erosion-control products also rebounded strongly.  
• Management expects resin supply to improve late 2022/early 2023, and believes unmet 2021 orders were not lost to competitors.  
• Polypropylene peaked above $1.40/lb in Q3-21 but is now trending near $1.00/lb; multiple price rounds have largely re-matched selling prices with current costs.  
• Scapa produced $24 million of operating profit, but approximately $6 million of its SG&A was booked at corporate, making the deal slightly dilutive to 2021 EPS. Initial cost-synergy targets were met; commercial synergies are now estimated in the “tens of millions” over time.  
• Over the next two years SWM targets AMS operating margins “15 %+,” implying a 400-bp uplift versus 2021.

## Engineered Papers (EP)

• Sales declined 4 % (volumes –2 %), reflecting expected LIP inventory normalization and negative mix; currency was a small tailwind.  
• Adjusted operating profit fell 18 % to $109 million; margin slid 360 bp to 21.5 %.  
• Wood-pulp costs and energy added > $15 million in headwinds. Annual customer contracts had reset before pulp spiked, leaving SWM exposed for most of 2021.  
• Recent negotiations have embedded semi-annual pulp pass-throughs and energy/transport surcharges, which should stabilize margin variability.  
• Heat-not-burn paper sales nearly doubled to > $25 million, and the company launched “Botani,” a hemp- and botanical-fiber platform aimed at non-tobacco applications, positioning EP for incremental growth despite secular attrition in conventional papers.

## 2022 Guidance & Outlook

Management forecasts a “significant profit rebound”:

• Adjusted EPS: $3.50–$3.95 (up as much as 27 %).  
• Adjusted EBITDA: +20 % to +30 %.  
• Sales: double-digit percentage growth, aided by a full year of Scapa.  
• Tax rate: low- to mid-20 % range (up year on year, hence EPS growth trails EBITDA growth).  
• Free cash flow: ~ $100 million (vs $19 million in 2021), on $45–55 million of capex and normalized working-capital movements.  
• Quarterly cadence: EPS improvement versus Q4-21 each quarter; Q2-Q4 expected to average ≈ $1.00/sh; Q1 faces the toughest comparison.  
• Net leverage projected to decline toward 4× EBITDA by year-end 2022.

Segment expectations: AMS is projected to deliver “very strong” profit growth and margin expansion as pricing, cost deflation and Scapa synergies take hold; Scapa will be “solidly accretive” for the full year. EP profits are guided at least flat with upside tied to innovation uptake; price mechanisms and ongoing efficiencies should offset remaining inflation.

## Strategic & Qualitative Highlights

• Scapa materially broadens SWM’s technology base, triples health-care revenue, and lifts the overall portfolio such that ~70 % of company sales now address end-markets growing at or above GDP.  
• Innovation priorities include: specialty tapes leveraging EP assets; fiber-based sustainable packaging; paper-based cigarette filters; botanical and hemp wrappers/fillers; and new air-filtration media.  
• Management views customer relationships as strengthened by SWM’s supply-chain resilience; multiple customers granted out-of-cycle price relief or incremental volume to sustain the partnership.  
• Less than 1 % of revenue is exposed to Russia/Ukraine; the primary risk from that conflict is indirect (energy and freight).  
• An inaugural ESG report was published, and the company unveiled refreshed branding under the purpose statement “Finding ways to improve everyday life.”

Despite a 2021 earnings shortfall, SWM exits the year with pricing restored to cost parity, a deeper innovation pipeline, constructive contract structures and a more growth-weighted mix. Management believes these elements, together with a normalization of working capital, will translate into higher margins, stronger cash generation and gradual deleveraging in 2022 and beyond.
