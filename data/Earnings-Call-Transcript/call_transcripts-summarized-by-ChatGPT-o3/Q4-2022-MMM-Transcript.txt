# 3M Company – Q4 2022 Earnings Call Summary

3M closed 2022 under mounting macro-pressures but kept its balance sheet solid, preserved cash generation and laid out a conservative 2023 plan that assumes continued weakness in consumer-facing and electronics markets, a sharp step-down in COVID–respirator revenue and persistent foreign-exchange headwinds. The company is accelerating restructuring—beginning with the elimination of about 2,500 manufacturing positions—and is preparing both for a late-2023 / early-2024 spin-off of its Health Care business and for a complete exit from PFAS manufacturing by year-end 2025.

### Fourth-Quarter 2022 Results  
• Reported sales were $8.08 billion, down 6.2 percent year-on-year (-1.6 pp from divestitures and ‑5.0 pp from currency).  
• Organic growth was +0.4 percent versus the prior year; excluding disposable respirators and the Russia exit, organic growth would have been about +3 percent.  
• Adjusted operating margin was 19.1 percent, down 30 bp, as 110 bp of self-help actions were offset by inflation (-140 bp) and the respirator/Russia drag (-70 bp).  
• Adjusted EPS of $2.28 missed consensus by $0.09 and declined 7 percent year-over-year.  
• Free cash flow was $1.7 billion (conversion 131 percent) aided by a $250 million inventory drawdown.  
• Net debt fell 4 percent year-over-year to $12 billion; net-debt-to-EBITDA is 1.4×.

Segment detail (organic growth, Q4 sales, adjusted operating margin):  
– Safety & Industrial: +1.3 % ($2.7 B; 22.4 % margin). Excluding disposable respirators, growth was +7.5 %.  
– Transportation & Electronics: +1.4 % ($2.1 B; 17.8 % margin). Auto OEM grew mid-teens; electronics fell 10 %.  
– Health Care: +1.9 % ($2.0 B; 20.6 % margin, EBITDA ≈29 %). Medical solutions declined, elective procedures ~90 % of pre-COVID levels; separation & purification up high-single digits.  
– Consumer: -5.7 % ($1.2 B; 17.9 % margin) on broad U.S. retail destocking and weaker discretionary spend.

### Full-Year 2022 Snapshot  
• Revenue: $34.2 billion (-3 % reported, +1 % organic; +3 % organic ex-respirator/Russia).  
• Adjusted EPS: $10.10.  
• Adjusted free cash flow: $4.7 billion; 82 % conversion.  
• Dividends paid: $3.4 billion; share repurchases: $1.5 billion; total shareholder return: $4.8 billion.  
• Shares outstanding reduced by 16 million via the Food Safety split-off.  
• Capital spending: $1.75 billion (+9 %).  
• Key strategic actions: divested Food Safety (≈$1 billion proceeds), announced PFAS exit (Q4 charge $0.8 billion toward an expected $1.3–$2.3 billion total), advanced Health Care spin.

### 2023 Guidance (all figures on the new PFAS-adjusted basis)  
Sales:  
• Organic growth –3 % to 0 % (price up low single digits; volume down low-/mid-single digits).  
• Foreign-exchange headwind –1 % to –2 %; divestitures –1 %.  
• Disposable respirator revenue expected to fall $450–$550 million (≈-4 pp to Safety & Industrial).  
Adjusted EPS: $8.50–$9.00. Guidance embeds:  
  – $0.30–$0.45 hit from respirator decline and Russia exit;  
  – $0.10–$0.20 from FX;  
  – $0.15 from 2022 divestitures;  
  – $0.20–$0.35 from carry-over inflation in raw materials, logistics and energy;  
  – Tax rate 18–19 %; non-operating items flat to –$0.10 (pension expense +$125 million).  
Free cash-flow conversion: 90–100 %.  
Capital spending: $1.5–$1.8 billion (≈$200 million earmarked for water-stewardship tied to PFAS exit).  

Segment outlook:  
• Safety & Industrial: organic down low single digits; respirator decline ≈-4 pp.  
• Transportation & Electronics (ex-PFAS impact): down mid-single digits to flat; auto builds +4 %, electronics “significantly” down on weak TVs/tablets/phones and LCD→OLED shift.  
• Health Care: up low- to mid-single digits; gradual elective-procedure recovery.  
• Consumer: down low single digits to flat; assumes retailer destocking eases in 2H-23.

### First-Quarter 2023 Preview  
• Adjusted sales expected at $7.2–$7.6 billion, down 10–15 % versus the PFAS-adjusted $8.5 billion prior-year base.  
  – Organic down low to mid-single digits; FX –3–4 pp; respirator/Russia –3–4 pp; divestitures –1 pp.  
• Adjusted EPS: $1.25–$1.65, including a $75–$100 million (≈$0.10–$0.15) restructuring charge and a ~19 % tax rate.  
• Q1 will bear the heaviest year-on-year headwinds; management expects sequential improvement as comps ease and supply chains heal.

### Restructuring and Cost-Savings Actions  
• About 2,500 global manufacturing roles are being eliminated to align capacity with softer demand.  
• Management is “taking a deeper look at everything”—supply-chain simplification, discretionary spending freezes, tighter hiring and identification of stranded costs ahead of the Health Care spin. Additional actions and associated savings targets will be disclosed as decisions are finalized.

### Strategic & Legal Updates  
• PFAS: manufacturing exit by end-2025; PFAS-related revenue and profit will be excluded from non-GAAP reporting beginning in 2023.  
• Health Care spin-off: execution team in place; target completion by late-2023 / early-2024; estimated transaction cost $1.0–$1.5 billion (largely excluded from adjusted earnings).  
• Litigation: combat arms mediation continues; PFAS lawsuits being defended or settled case-by-case.  
• Innovation highlights include robotics for auto paint repair, Scotch Cushion Lock sustainable packaging, advanced wound-care systems and thermal barriers for electric-vehicle batteries (EV platform sales grew 30 % organically in 2022).

### Balance-Sheet & Capital-Allocation Priorities  
3M retains an A-rated balance sheet, net-debt/EBITDA of 1.4× and robust liquidity, enabling continued investment in growth, productivity and sustainability while sustaining its dividend (currently yielding about 5 %). Management reiterated its commitment to a “strong balance-sheet and capital-structure” philosophy through the PFAS exit and the pending separation of Health Care.

### Management’s Closing Perspective  
CEO Mike Roman emphasized that 2023 is a pivotal execution year. Despite a “very tough start,” he expects volume, margin and cash-flow metrics to improve as the year progresses, helped by supply-chain healing, aggressive self-help actions and easing year-over-year comparisons. Roman underscored confidence that exiting 2023, 3M will be “a stronger, leaner and more focused company” positioned for long-term growth in attractive markets such as automotive electrification, industrial automation, biopharma processing and home improvement.
