# NVIDIA FY22 Q4 Earnings Call – Comprehensive Financial & Strategic Summary

NVIDIA ended fiscal 2022 with its strongest quarter and year on record, driven by broad-based demand across Gaming, Data Center and Professional Visualization, while continuing to expand its software and automotive franchises.

NVIDIA reported fourth-quarter FY22 (period ended 30 Jan 2022) revenue of **$7.64 billion**, up **53 % year-on-year** and **8 % sequentially**, setting quarterly records for both the company and its three largest platforms. Full-year revenue reached **$26.91 billion**, a **61 % increase** on top of FY21’s 53 % growth.

## Segment Performance, Q4 FY22  

• **Gaming** revenue was **$3.42 billion** (+6 % Q/Q; +37 % Y/Y). Both desktop and laptop GeForce RTX 30-Series sales set records. FY22 Gaming sales were **$12.46 billion** (+61 %).  
• **Data Center** revenue hit a record **$3.26 billion** (+11 % Q/Q; +71 % Y/Y), bringing FY22 Data Center sales to **$10.61 billion** (+58 %). Growth was led by AI compute products; hyperscale/cloud revenue more than doubled Y/Y and vertical-industry revenue grew strong double digits. Inference revenue more than tripled Y/Y.  
• **Professional Visualization** delivered **$643 million** (+11 % Q/Q; +109 % Y/Y); FY22 revenue doubled to **$2.11 billion**. Demand came from hybrid-workstation build-outs, RTX-based design, rendering and AI workloads, and initial Omniverse Enterprise licenses.  
• **Automotive** revenue declined to **$125 million** (-7 % Q/Q; ‑14 % Y/Y) but FY22 still rose 6 % to **$566 million**. Management expects sequential growth in Q1 FY23 with a “more meaningful inflection” in 2H FY23 as Orin-based platforms ramp.  
• **OEM & Other** includes Cryptocurrency Mining Processors (CMP); CMP revenue was **$24 million** in Q4. Nearly all Ampere gaming GPUs are “Lite Hash Rate” to protect GeForce supply for gamers.

## Profitability & Cash Items (Q4 GAAP unless noted)  

Management gave detailed margin and expense guidance but did not recite Q4 EPS metrics on the call.  
• **Q1 FY23 outlook:** revenue **$8.1 billion ± 2 %**; GAAP gross margin **65.2 % ± 50 bp** (non-GAAP **67.0 % ± 50 bp**).  
• **GAAP operating expenses** in Q1 are expected at **$3.55 billion**, including a **$1.36 billion** write-off of the terminated Arm acquisition; **non-GAAP opex** will be **$1.6 billion**.  
• **Other income/expense**: approximately **$55 million expense** GAAP & non-GAAP.  
• **Non-GAAP tax rate**: **11 %–13 % ± 1 %**.  
• **Capital expenditures**: **$350-400 million** for Q1.  
• No new debt figures were provided; the company holds **over $20 billion in cash**.

## Supply Chain & Inventory Commitments  

NVIDIA remains **demand-constrained** across GPUs, networking and some substrates, though the operations team expanded its manufacturing footprint substantially. Management expects sequential supply increases each quarter of FY23 and confirmed four consecutive quarters of >30 % sequential growth in inventory purchase obligations, which now exceed quarterly revenue, reflecting both tight industry capacity and confidence in future demand as multiple new product ramps (e.g., Orin, Grace) approach.

## Strategic & Product Highlights  

1. **Data Center Applications:** Growth is propelled by large-language models, conversational AI, recommender systems and cloud graphics. NVIDIA’s full-stack approach (A100 GPUs, Triton & Triton Inference Server, Mellanox networking, DGX systems) continues to set MLPerf training records; Meta’s new AI supercomputer uses >6,000 A100s, expanding to 16,000.  
2. **Networking:** Demand for InfiniBand (Quantum) and Ethernet (Spectrum) switches and BlueField DPUs is strong. Supply limits curbed revenue, but higher capacity is expected through FY23. Mellanox integration accelerates road-maps; NVIDIA now sells systems with up to eight InfiniBand links per DGX node.  
3. **Software Monetisation:** FY22 marked the commercial launch of three subscription/licensing franchises:  
   • **NVIDIA AI Enterprise** (per-server licence) for VMware and mainstream IT;  
   • **Omniverse Enterprise & Cloud** (per connector/seat) for digital-twin collaboration;  
   • **NVIDIA DRIVE AV / IX** (shared lifetime software revenue with OEMs).  
   Early pipelines are “building” and management sees software as a meaningful long-term margin and revenue driver.  
4. **Automotive Partnerships:** Announced multi-year, fleet-wide deal with **Jaguar Land Rover** beginning 2025 using DRIVE Orin, Hyperion sensor suite and end-to-end NVIDIA software stack. Similar revenue-sharing model to existing Mercedes-Benz agreement; management estimates combined NVIDIA-powered fleets could exceed **10 million vehicles in a decade**.  
5. **Arm Transaction:** NVIDIA officially terminated the planned Arm acquisition due to regulatory headwinds, booking the $1.36 billion breakup fee. Strategy remains intact: focus on accelerated computing, continue Arm architectural licence, and launch the **Grace Arm-based data-center CPU** in first half of 2023.  
6. **Gaming Ecosystem:** Steam’s concurrent users reached 28 million (+50 % in two years). Over 160 new RTX laptop designs and the RTX 3050 desktop GPU launched; NVIDIA’s Reflex and DLSS adoption broadened across major titles. GeForce NOW cloud gaming expanded with Samsung and LG smart-TV integration and first Canadian data center; AT&T partnership brings 5G streaming.  
7. **Product Pipeline:** FY23 will see volume ramps of Orin SoCs, Grace CPUs, next-gen Ada Lovelace GPUs, and new high-speed networking products, all supported by increased supply agreements.

## Management Outlook & Tone  

• **Q1 FY23 growth will be led by Data Center** with Gaming also up sequentially despite typical seasonality; Automotive rebounds.  
• NVIDIA expects to remain supply-constrained but to **“improve every quarter.”**  
• The company views AI adoption as still **early-innings** (≈10 % enterprise penetration), forecasting substantial long-term growth as every server will run AI workloads and every industry builds 3D digital twins.  
• Software subscriptions and automotive services should **lift long-run gross margins** beyond already high levels.  
• Capital allocation strategy is under review now that the Arm cash remains on hand; management reiterated enthusiasm for organic investment but left the door open to inorganic opportunities.
