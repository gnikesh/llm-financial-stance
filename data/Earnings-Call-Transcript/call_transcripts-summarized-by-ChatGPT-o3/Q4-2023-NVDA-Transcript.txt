# NVIDIA Fiscal Q4 2023 Earnings Call – Comprehensive Financial & Strategic Summary

NVIDIA closed fiscal 2023 with revenue of $27.0 billion, essentially flat year-on-year, after a fourth-quarter top line of $6.05 billion that grew 2 percent sequentially but fell 21 percent versus the prior-year quarter.  Fiscal-year GAAP EPS was not disclosed on the call, but management highlighted gross-margin recovery, disciplined operating-expense control and continued shareholder returns.

Fourth-quarter GAAP gross margin rebounded to 63.3 percent and non-GAAP gross margin to 66.1 percent (vs. full-year margins of 56.9 percent and 59.2 percent respectively), helped by richer product mix (notably the new Hopper H100 data-center GPU) and waning channel-inventory charges.  GAAP operating expenses were flat sequentially at roughly $2.54 billion (up 21 percent YoY), and non-GAAP opex dipped 1 percent to $1.78 billion.  The company repurchased shares and paid dividends totaling $1.15 billion in Q4, leaving about $7 billion on its authorization through December 2023.  Balance-sheet metrics such as net debt were not detailed, but capital-expenditure guidance was set at $350-400 million for Q1 and $1.1-1.3 billion for full-year FY24.

Segment performance remained mixed:  

• Data Center revenue was $3.62 billion, down 6 percent sequentially yet up 11 percent year-on-year; full-year Data Center sales climbed 41 percent to $15 billion.  Hyperscale demand grew sequentially but undershot expectations as some cloud providers paused year-end build-outs.  Roughly 40 percent of trailing-four-quarter Data Center revenue came from CSPs.  China softness and general-purpose CPU infrastructure digestion weighed on Q4, but management expects Data Center to grow both sequentially and year-on-year in Q1 FY24 and to accelerate thereafter.  

• Gaming revenue rebounded 16 percent sequentially to $1.83 billion but remained 46 percent below the prior-year period; fiscal-year Gaming revenue slipped 27 percent to $9.07 billion.  Channel-inventory correction is “largely behind,” and the Ada Lovelace-based RTX 4090/4080/4070 Ti are selling through rapidly.  Ada laptop GPUs—NVIDIA’s biggest mobile generational leap—entered retail in Q1, and GeForce NOW surpassed 25 million members with an “Ultimate” tier powered by cloud RTX 4080s.  NVIDIA also inked a 10-year pact to stream Microsoft Xbox PC titles (including future Activision content) in GeForce NOW.  

• Professional Visualization posted $226 million, up 13 percent sequentially but down 65 percent year-over-year; the channel-correction drag should subside in 1H FY24.  Omniverse downloads approached 300,000 with 185 third-party connectors.  

• Automotive achieved a record $294 million, rising 17 percent sequentially and 135 percent YoY; FY23 Auto revenue reached $903 million, up 60 percent.  Foxconn will manufacture DRIVE Orin ECUs, while Mercedes-Benz targets “single-digit-billion-euro” annual software revenue mid-decade (shared 50/50 with NVIDIA).  TÜV SÜD certified NVIDIA DRIVE OS for functional safety.

Networking remained a bright spot: demand for Quantum-2 400 Gbps InfiniBand and 400 Gbps Spectrum-4 Ethernet accelerated, fuelled by large-scale AI clusters.  Hopper’s NVLink and transformer engine enable up to 9× faster large-model training and 30× faster inference versus A100; Q4 H100 sales already exceeded A100 shipments for the quarter despite being only the second quarter of availability.

Management characterises generative AI as a once-in-a-generation inflection comparable to the iPhone.  ChatGPT’s viral adoption triggered “a sense of urgency” among enterprises, CSPs and software vendors, materially lifting NVIDIA’s demand outlook over the past 60-90 days.  To democratise AI “factories,” the company unveiled a new services-centric business model:  

• **NVIDIA DGX Cloud** – fully hosted DGX supercomputer clusters, now live on Oracle Cloud Infrastructure and coming to Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud and others.  
• **NVIDIA AI Enterprise 3.0** – subscription software stack (~$6,000 per CPU socket for on-prem licenses; higher on consumption-based cloud) now supporting 50+ frameworks; annualised software revenue is already “hundreds of millions” and set new records in Q4.  
• **NeMo and BioNeMo** foundation-model services are in early access.  

Although competitive accelerators exist, NVIDIA stresses the universality of CUDA, end-to-end workload acceleration (including pre- and post-processing) and lowest total operating cost.  The company maintains its previously stated total addressable market of roughly $300 billion for hardware and $300 billion for software but contends the generative-AI wave will let it reach that TAM “sooner than later.”

For Q1 FY24 (ending April 2023), NVIDIA guides: revenue of $6.5 billion ± 2 percent driven by sequential growth in all four platforms (led by Data Center and Gaming); GAAP gross margin of 64.1 percent ± 50 bps and non-GAAP gross margin of 66.5 percent ± 50 bps; GAAP opex ~$2.53 billion and non-GAAP opex ~$1.78 billion; other income ~$50 million; tax rate 13 percent ± 1 percent.  Management plans to hold operating expenses “relatively flat” near current levels.

CEO Jensen Huang reiterated a long-term ambition to improve AI-training efficiency another million-fold over the next decade through advances in chips, interconnects, systems and algorithms, and he foresees “AI factories” proliferating worldwide as enterprises turn proprietary data into continuous, revenue-generating intelligence.

Key upcoming events include the Morgan Stanley Technology Conference (March 6), Cowen Healthcare Conference (March 7) and the spring GTC developer conference (virtual keynote March 21), at which NVIDIA promises further announcements on new chips, systems, software, CUDA applications and expanded AI/Omniverse ecosystems.
