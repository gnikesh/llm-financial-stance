# NVIDIA Q4 FY 2024 Earnings Call – Financial Summary and Key Take-aways

NVIDIA ended fiscal 2024 with another series of record figures driven by unrelenting demand for accelerated computing and generative AI workloads. Fourth-quarter revenue reached $22.1 billion, exceeding the company’s own $20 billion outlook by more than $2 billion, rising 22 percent sequentially and 265 percent year on year. Full-year revenue climbed 126 percent to $60.9 billion.

Data-center activity remained the main growth engine. Segment revenue for FY 2024 ballooned to $47.5 billion—more than triple the prior year—while fourth-quarter data-center sales hit an all-time high of $18.4 billion, up 27 percent versus Q3 and up 409 percent versus last year’s quarter. Compute revenue within the segment increased more than five-fold and networking revenue tripled as the Hopper GPU platform and InfiniBand networking became the industry’s de-facto standard for AI infrastructure. Management estimates that roughly 40 percent of data-center revenue over the past year already comes from AI inference, not just training—evidence that generative AI models are moving into production. Supply of the current Hopper generation is improving, yet demand for next-generation parts such as the H200 (whose initial shipments begin in Q2 and which nearly doubles H100 inference performance) is expected to outstrip supply for some time.

AI adoption broadened across virtually every customer category. Hyperscale and cloud providers still accounted for more than half of data-center revenue, but consumer-internet firms, enterprise-software vendors, automotive OEMs, healthcare companies and sovereign governments are all building “AI factories.” Automotive data-center revenue alone exceeded $1 billion in FY 2024. Outside the United States and China, “sovereign AI” build-outs are emerging as a new demand driver as countries train large-language models on domestic data. By contrast, Chinese data-center sales fell sharply after the October U.S. export-control tightening; China contributed only a mid-single-digit percentage of Q4 data-center revenue, and management expects a similar mix in Q1 while it ramps compliant product alternatives.

NVIDIA’s networking franchise surpassed a $13 billion annualized run rate; Quantum InfiniBand shipments rose more than 5× year on year. The company is now entering Ethernet for AI clusters via the new Spectrum-X platform, which will start shipping this quarter and is said to deliver 1.6× the AI networking performance of traditional Ethernet. Software and subscription services, led by NVIDIA AI Enterprise and DGX Cloud, have reached a $1 billion annualized run rate, underscoring the growing recurring-revenue component of the portfolio.

In Gaming, revenue of $2.87 billion was essentially flat sequentially but grew 56 percent from the prior year; FY 2024 gaming revenue advanced 15 percent to $10.45 billion. The recently launched GeForce RTX 40 “Super” GPUs and a wave of RTX 40-series AI laptops are seeing strong initial sell-through. Professional Visualization generated Q4 revenue of $463 million, up 11 percent sequentially and 105 percent year on year (FY revenue was $1.55 billion, up 1 percent). Automotive posted Q4 revenue of $281 million, rising 8 percent quarter on quarter but slipping 4 percent year on year; the segment nonetheless crossed $1 billion for the first time on a full-year basis ($1.09 billion, up 21 percent).

Gross margins reached unprecedented levels: GAAP margin expanded to 76.0 percent and non-GAAP to 76.7 percent, helped by richer product mix and favorable component costs; management cautions that these cost tailwinds are temporary and projects gross margin in “the mid-70s” for the remainder of FY 2025. GAAP operating expenses rose 6 percent sequentially (non-GAAP +9 percent) due to head-count growth and higher infrastructure spend. NVIDIA returned $2.8 billion to shareholders in Q4 via share repurchases and dividends, bringing FY 2024 capital returned to $9.9 billion, of which $9.5 billion was buybacks.

For the first quarter of fiscal 2025, NVIDIA guides for revenue of $24 billion plus or minus 2 percent, implying continued sequential growth in Data Center and Professional Visualization partially offset by seasonal softness in Gaming. GAAP gross margin is expected at 76.3 percent and non-GAAP at 77.0 percent (±50 bp), again benefitting from short-term component cost tailwinds before normalizing. GAAP and non-GAAP operating expenses are projected at roughly $3.5 billion and $2.5 billion, respectively, and for the full fiscal year those figures are slated to rise in the mid-30 percent range as NVIDIA invests for continued expansion. Other income is estimated at about $250 million and the tax rate at 17 percent, both GAAP and non-GAAP.

CEO Jensen Huang reiterated that the world’s $1 trillion installed base of data-center infrastructure is at a “tipping point,” shifting from general-purpose CPUs to accelerated computing and generative-AI “factories.” He expects those twin transitions to double global data-center capacity over the next five years, translating into an annual market worth “hundreds of billions” of dollars. Management therefore remains confident in sustained growth through calendar 2024, 2025 and beyond despite near-term supply constraints on new products. 

The company will provide further product and platform updates at its in-person GTC conference beginning March 18 in San Jose.
